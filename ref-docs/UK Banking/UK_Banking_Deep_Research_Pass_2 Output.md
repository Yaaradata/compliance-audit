## SECTION B — UK Supervisory Style and Behaviour Brief (Activity 2)

### B.1 The Dual-Regulator Reality

The UK has two banking supervisors operating concurrently over every authorised bank: the Prudential Regulation Authority (PRA), part of the Bank of England, and the Financial Conduct Authority (FCA). Both regulators have statutory powers over the same firm — this is not a federal/state split, not a functional split (such as US prudential vs. consumer protection delegated to different bodies), and not a sequenced relationship. Both supervisors operate continuously and simultaneously.

**Division of focus.** The PRA's statutory objective is the safety and soundness of regulated firms and the protection of policyholders for insurers; in banking practice this means capital, liquidity, governance of risk, large exposures, recovery and resolution, IRB models, ICAAP/ILAAP, and the firm's resilience under stress. The FCA's objectives are consumer protection, market integrity, and effective competition; in banking practice this means customer treatment, conduct of business, financial crime prevention, market abuse, complaints handling, Consumer Duty outcomes, and the conduct elements of governance.

**Where they overlap.** Senior management approval (SMCR) is jointly operated — most SMFs (SMF1 CEO, SMF2 CFO, SMF3 Executive Director, SMF4 CRO, SMF9 Chair, SMF10 Chair Risk Committee, SMF11 Chair Audit Committee, SMF24 Chief Operations) are designated by both regulators; SMF16 Compliance Oversight and SMF17 MLRO are FCA-approved; SMF5 Head of Internal Audit is PRA-approved. Operational Resilience under PS21/3 / SS1/21 is a joint regime requiring coordinated firm response. The CTPs regime under FSMA 2023 is operated jointly with the Bank of England as the third co-supervisor.

**The supervisory cadence is regulator-specific.** PRA categorises banks 1–4 by potential financial impact and supervisory intensity. Cat 1 firms (the largest 7 systemic banks) receive intense, near-continuous supervision with quarterly Periodic Summary Meetings (PSMs) and annual ICAAP/ILAAP reviews. Cat 2 firms (significant non-systemic) receive an annual or more frequent PSM cycle. Cat 3 firms (medium) receive less intense periodic supervision typically organised on an annual or biennial cycle. Cat 4 firms (smaller) receive baseline portfolio-level supervision. Mid-sized UK banks (the equivalent of the US $10–50bn baseline in this research) typically sit in Cat 2 or upper Cat 3.

The FCA operates a portfolio-and-firm-specific supervisory model: the largest banks have a named Supervisor and dedicated team; mid-sized banks may have a named Supervisor or be managed within a portfolio team. The cadence is shaped by perceived risk and the FCA's annual Business Plan priorities.

**Where PRA and FCA create tension.** Most live tension areas in mid-2020s supervision: (a) the Consumer Duty fair-value assessment may push prices down on certain products in ways the PRA prudential capital position cannot easily absorb, particularly for thinner-margin products; (b) SMF approval timing — PRA may take longer to approve dual-regulated SMFs, affecting succession planning; (c) operational resilience overlap — the PRA's continuity-of-services lens and the FCA's customer-harm lens converge on IBS impact tolerances but with different framings; (d) when the regulators disagree about a bank's risk posture (rare but real), the firm must respond to both — the MoU between PRA and FCA aims to coordinate but does not eliminate the burden on the firm.

**What this means for compliance function design.** The UK compliance function must maintain two regulatory relationship maps, not one. The same incident may produce a PRA notification (under PRA Notifications Rulebook), an FCA notification (under SUP 15), and an Internal Audit assurance pathway; SMF accountability for the notifications may sit with different SMFs. The platform implication: every incident, finding, and obligation must carry a regulator-attribution metadata field — the supervisor that cares about it shapes the evidence format.

### B.2 The Supervisory Hot Zones (2024–2026)

The following are the live supervisory priorities, each traceable to specific regulatory communications and enforcement signals.

**B.2.1 Consumer Duty — outcomes evidence and fair value.** *Source signals:* FCA Dear CEO letter "Implementing the Consumer Duty in [sector]" series July 2024 onwards; FCA portfolio letters following the 31 July 2023 (open products) deadline; FCA Annual Report 2024–25; Consumer Duty Board Champion guidance. *What the regulator looks for:* documented Fair Value Assessments with consequential action; outcomes monitoring with customer-segment data; vulnerable customer identification rate and outcomes; communications testing evidence (not just "we ran clarity workshops"); the firm's Consumer Duty Annual Report. *What banks are most likely getting wrong:* FVAs that produce paper but no pricing change; outcome monitoring that does not distinguish vulnerable from non-vulnerable customer outcomes; communications testing that uses comprehension surveys with low response rates; a Board Champion role without delegated authority. *Evidence format expected:* outcomes data over time-series at customer-segment granularity; FVA documentation refresh cycle; explicit linkage from poor outcomes to product/process changes.

**B.2.2 Operational Resilience — March 2025 deadline aftermath and the CTP regime.** *Source signals:* PRA SS1/21 / FCA PS21/3 31 March 2025 compliance deadline; PRA / FCA / BoE policy statements on the CTPs regime under FSMA 2023; supervisory visits to Cat 1 / Cat 2 firms; published thematic findings on IBS mapping quality. *What the regulator looks for:* IBS register completeness and quality; impact tolerances based on operational evidence (not aspirational); severe-but-plausible scenario tests covering cyber, third-party failure, technology failure, and combined scenarios; mapping accuracy verifiable against system telemetry; remediation programmes for tolerance-breaches. *What banks are most likely getting wrong:* impact tolerances set without scenario validation; IBS mapping not refreshed after process change; concentration risk in cloud (AWS, Azure, Google Cloud) under-quantified; insufficient SMF (PR Z holder) engagement with the self-assessment. *Evidence format expected:* annual board-approved Self-Assessment; scenario test programmes with design rationale; CTP dependency mapping integrated with the firm's resilience framework from the point of CTP designation. The supervisory expectation following the March 2025 compliance deadline has shifted from "are you working towards this?" to "demonstrate you are within tolerance." Firms that submitted self-assessments citing ongoing remediation without tested tolerance evidence are receiving supervisory challenge. The CTP regime applies from mid-2025 onward to designated providers; banks are expected to have mapped their CTP dependencies and integrated CTP incident-reporting flows before the regime becomes enforceable. AWS, Microsoft Azure, and a small number of critical payments infrastructure providers are the anticipated initial designees.
 
### B.2.3 Financial Crime Systems and Controls Enforcement
 
*Source signals:* FCA Final Notice — Starling Bank Ltd (October 2024, £29m fine for inadequate financial crime controls); FCA Final Notice — NatWest Group plc (September 2021, £264.8m, the first criminal conviction of a bank for AML failures); FCA Dear CEO letter on financial crime systems and controls (March 2022, followed by portfolio letters 2023–2024); FCA Financial Crime Annual Report 2024. *What the regulator looks for:* documented firm-wide risk assessment that genuinely drives controls, not a generic sector-template; customer risk grading applied consistently and evidenced at the point of decision; transaction monitoring calibrated against a documented methodology with false-positive measurement; DAML consent process with documented moratorium management; MLRO Annual Report reviewed at board level with consequential action. *What banks are most likely getting wrong at mid-sized firms:* transaction monitoring rules that have never been retested against the current customer book since initial vendor implementation; customer risk grades assigned at onboarding and then left static for years; MLRO Annual Reports that are compliance-authored summaries rather than board-engaged governance documents; correspondent banking files lacking meaningful respondent assessment; offshore captive teams executing SAR investigations without sufficient senior oversight or quality assurance by the UK MLRO. *Evidence format expected:* FRA with version history and board approval; transaction monitoring methodology document with false-positive rationale; SAR MI broken down by referral source, typology, and disposition time; MLRO Annual Report with board-approved response.
 
### B.2.4 APP Fraud Reimbursement — New Regulatory Accountability
 
*Source signals:* PSR PS23/3 (June 2023 policy statement); PSR implementation communications (July–October 2024); FCA Dear CEO on operational resilience of fraud prevention (various 2023–2024); Pay.UK Reimbursement Rules v1.0 (October 2024). *What the regulator looks for:* reimbursement workflow capable of deciding claims within 5 business days; vulnerable customer identification integrated into the claims assessment; receiving PSP account-monitoring capable of detecting mule-account activity triggering inter-PSP recovery; Consumer Standard of Caution evidence for every denied claim; PSR fraud data reporting against the mandatory template. *What banks are most likely getting wrong:* denying claims on vague "gross negligence" grounds without evidencing the high-bar test; failing to identify vulnerable customers during initial claim assessment; receiving PSPs not yet operationally capable of processing recovery claims promptly; inadequate staff training on distinguishing ineligible transactions (e.g., civil disputes, investment scams outside scope) from in-scope APP fraud. *Evidence format expected:* reimbursement decision log per claim with rationale; vulnerable customer assessment record; inter-PSP settlement data to Pay.UK; PSR data submission per reporting cycle. *The P&L dimension:* This hot zone is distinguished by direct, computable P&L impact — reimbursement costs are materialising in monthly management accounts, creating a commercial urgency absent from most compliance obligations.
 
### B.2.5 AI and Machine Learning in Banking — FCA CP24/2 / Model Risk
 
*Source signals:* FCA Discussion Paper DP22/4 (March 2022, AI/ML in financial services); FCA CP24/2 (proposed guidance on AI and anti-discrimination in financial services, May 2024); PRA SS1/23 (Model Risk Management Principles for Banks, effective May 2024); FCA / Alan Turing Institute joint report on the use of AI in financial services (2022). *What the regulator looks for:* model inventory that includes all AI/ML models, not just quantitative risk models; pre-deployment independent validation for material AI/ML models; ongoing performance monitoring with alert thresholds; Consumer Duty fair-treatment implications assessed for algorithmic credit and pricing decisions; explainability requirements met for adverse credit decisions (CONC cross-reference). *What banks are most likely getting wrong:* AI/ML models deployed by technology teams without passing through the model risk governance process; credit models with demographic bias undetected due to inadequate validation methodology; vendor black-box models accepted without contractual access to sufficient model information for governance; IFRS 9 ECL models under-assessed against SS1/23 given the expanded scope.
 
### B.2.6 Vulnerable Customers — FCA FG21/1 Operational Embedding
 
*Source signals:* FCA FG21/1 (Guidance for firms on the fair treatment of vulnerable customers, February 2021); FCA Consumer Duty portfolio letters 2024 citing vulnerable-customer identification gaps; FCA thematic review on treatment of customers in vulnerable circumstances (ongoing as of 2025). *What the regulator looks for:* vulnerability identification embedded at every touchpoint (onboarding, servicing, complaints, collections), not only at call centres; staff trained and empowered to act on vulnerability indicators; outcome monitoring comparing vulnerable and non-vulnerable customer outcomes across the four Consumer Duty outcomes; product design that serves customers with characteristics of vulnerability, not just "average" customers; the firm's definition of vulnerability evidenced in policy and training. *What banks are most likely getting wrong:* vulnerability flags that exist in the CRM but do not propagate to downstream processes; call-centre staff identifying vulnerability but having no escalation pathway with material consequence; collections practices inconsistent with FG21/1 despite nominal Consumer Duty compliance. *Evidence format expected:* segmented outcome data by vulnerability characteristic; vulnerability identification rate by channel; complaints data by vulnerability status.
 
### B.2.7 Senior Manager Personal Accountability Enforcement
 
*Source signals:* FCA Final Notice — Carlos Abarca (CIO, TSB, fined £81,620, December 2022, re 2018 IT migration failures); FCA Final Notice — various SMF enforcement actions 2020–2024; PRA / FCA joint paper on individual accountability and governance (2023 SMCR review findings); FCA press release on SMF enforcement increases. *What the regulator looks for:* evidence that the SMF took concrete steps before the event, not post-hoc rationalisation; delegation documented with oversight evidence; board minutes attributing decisions to named SMFs; escalation audit trails showing the SMF received, considered, and acted on relevant information. *What banks are most likely getting wrong:* SoRs drafted as broad job descriptions rather than specific accountability delineations; the MRM not updated when responsibilities change; SMFs relying on "I was never told" as a defence without having required information flows that would have told them; absence of a personal evidence file maintained contemporaneously rather than assembled retrospectively.
 
### B.2.8 Market Integrity — Transaction Reporting Quality and STOR
 
*Source signals:* FCA Market Watch newsletters #68–76 (2020–2024); FCA fines for transaction reporting errors (Deutsche Bank 2021, various 2022–2024); FCA Supervisory Strategy for markets oversight 2024–2025. *What the regulator looks for:* T+1 completeness and accuracy of transaction reports; LEI maintenance (annual renewal and entity validation); STOR quality and timeliness; best execution monitoring that identifies outlier trades; market data controls preventing stale reference data from producing false-clean reports. *What banks are most likely getting wrong:* LEI expiry causing transaction reporting failures at year-end; entity classification errors (financial counterparty vs. non-financial counterparty) affecting EMIR reporting; STOR process that relies on market surveillance alerts but does not capture human-initiated suspicious orders identified by voice brokers; best execution policies that have not been updated to reflect actual venue usage.

### B.3 The UK Compliance Moment of Truth — The S.166 Anatomy
 
**What s.166 is.** Section 166 FSMA 2000 gives the FCA and PRA the power to require an authorised firm to commission a report by a "skilled person" — typically a Big 4 accounting firm or specialist regulatory consultancy approved by the regulator — on any matter relevant to the exercise of the regulator's functions. The firm appoints the skilled person, the skilled person reports to the regulator (not the firm), and the firm pays. The firm cannot unilaterally discontinue the engagement.
 
**Trigger patterns.** The legal power is broad ("at any time"), but documented trigger patterns from published sources include: (a) a specific incident or failure — a significant data breach, an AML transaction that crystallised into a major case, a sustained complaints surge, a mis-selling allegation, a large operational failure — that causes the supervisor to need independent verification of the firm's own account; (b) a thematic review that identifies the firm as an outlier among peers without adequate self-explanation; (c) a supervisory visit or PSM that reveals inadequate management information or governance documentation; (d) persistent or unresolved findings from the regulator's own information requests (s.165 requests) where the supervisor does not trust the firm's responses; (e) a whistleblower referral of sufficient substance; and (f) a "well-run firms don't need s.166" failure where the firm voluntarily discloses a problem that the regulator decides warrants independent assessment before regulatory action.
 
**The appointment process operationally.** The regulator issues a requirement notice (or agreement for a voluntary review, which is functionally equivalent) specifying the scope. The regulator and firm together agree on a panel of approved skilled persons; the regulator has effective veto. The skilled person's appointment letter, scope document, and reporting arrangement are agreed before work begins — the regulator signs off on the scope. The firm's compliance and legal teams mobilise a response team within days; a project manager is designated; document preservation and collection begins. The average mobilisation time from requirement notice to first working session is one to three weeks.
 
**What banks experience during a s.166 review.** The skilled person team — which may run to 15–30 or more consultants for a complex review — arrives and immediately requests a document population that typically spans: all relevant policies, procedures, and governance documents; MI packs from the relevant function for the prior 12–24 months; board and committee papers covering the subject; system configurations and mapping; sample case files (often 50–150 individual cases re-reviewed); staff interview schedule across the organisational hierarchy; and IT access to relevant systems (sometimes including shadow access to the firm's GRC tooling). The evidence demand is not structured around what the firm considers appropriate evidence — it follows the skilled person's hypothesis about where the failures are. 
 
The review duration is typically 3–12 months for a mid-sized firm; major reviews can run 18+ months. Resource mobilisation is significant: a typical mid-sized bank s.166 will require 3–6 internal full-time equivalents in the relevant compliance function, plus legal and project management overlay. Cost to the firm (both skilled person fees and internal resource) is typically £500k–£5m for a focused review and can exceed £25m for a systemic review across a large function.
 
**What skilled persons consistently find.** From published thematic skilled person review outputs and FCA supervisory statements: (a) documentation drift — policies describe a control that the current operational workflow does not execute; (b) evidence scatter — control evidence is distributed across multiple systems with no aggregation; (c) governance formalism without substance — committee minutes show decisions were made but do not show the underlying MI that drove them; (d) management information that does not reach the right level — the CRO or MLRO receives aggregated summaries that mask population-level control failures; (e) offshore execution quality — UK-regulated controls executed by offshore captive teams without adequate oversight or quality assurance by the UK function; (f) training gaps — staff executing regulated processes without sufficient or sufficiently recent training; (g) model/rule configuration that was never validated post-deployment against the live population.
 
**Consequence spectrum.** At the low end: voluntary requirements — the firm agrees to remediate specific gaps within a timeframe, with the skilled person conducting a follow-up review to validate. In the middle: a Risk Mitigation Programme (RMP) imposed by the regulator specifying mandatory actions. At the higher end: a public Final Notice with financial penalty naming the firm and (potentially) individual SMFs. In the most severe cases: a business restriction (e.g., no new customer onboarding until remediation), enforcement proceedings against SMF individuals, or (rarely) a FSMA s.45 variation of permission removing regulated activity. The reputational consequence of a public-outcome s.166 is distinct from the financial consequence: FCA publishes Final Notices; the press coverage typically runs before the penalty is paid; peer banks, analysts, and counterparties read them.
 
**How this differs from a US regulatory exam.** A US OCC or Federal Reserve examination is: (a) regulator-resourced and regulator-run, not firm-paid; (b) confidential — exam findings are not routinely published; (c) structured around pre-defined CAMELS or risk categories; (d) cyclical rather than event-triggered for routine exams; (e) findings result in MRA (Matters Requiring Attention) or MRIA (Matters Requiring Immediate Attention) which are internal supervisory directives. A UK s.166 is: firm-paid, firm-resourced, regulator-scoped, delivered by a third party appointed with regulator approval, and findings feed into publicly visible regulatory action. The evidence burden on the bank is substantially higher because the firm cannot predict or shape the scope in the way a well-prepared firm can influence its posture for a scheduled exam. The platform implication: evidence must be assembled and navigable on an on-demand basis, not assembled cyclically in preparation for a known exam window.

### B.4 The Principles-Based Reality in Practice
 
The UK regulatory system is not rules-based in the US sense. The FCA Handbook contains specific rules (marked "R"), but these are scaffolded around overarching Principles (PRIN) that operate as freestanding obligations. A firm can comply with every specific rule and still breach PRIN 6 (treating customers fairly) or PRIN 12 (Consumer Duty) if outcomes are poor. The following six examples illustrate where professional judgement is the compliance instrument.
 
**B.4.1 PRIN 12 / Consumer Duty — Fair Value Assessment for an Overdraft Facility.** *The obligation:* the firm must conduct a fair value assessment demonstrating that the price paid by the target market bears fair value to the benefit received (PRIN 2A.3.4R). *What good judgement looks like:* the firm segments its overdraft users by actual usage pattern (unarranged vs. arranged, duration, recovery rate), maps the actual benefit received against the annual percentage rate, considers the availability of alternatives, and documents the pricing rationale including cross-subsidy effects; it then acts on the assessment — if the FVA shows poor value for a segment, the product is repriced, redesigned, or withdrawn. *Evidence of good judgement:* a documented FVA with customer segmentation data, the pricing rationale signed off at governance level, and a consequential action log showing what changed as a result. *What failure of judgement looks like:* the FCA's Dear CEO letters (July 2024) identified firms producing FVA documentation that concluded "fair value" regardless of pricing evidence, without supporting data; this is a failure of judgement, not a specific rule breach, and the FCA may pursue it as a PRIN 12 breach even where no individual rule was technically violated.
 
**B.4.2 SYSC 4.1.1R — Adequate Governance During Operational Change.** *The obligation:* a firm must have robust governance arrangements including a clear organisational structure with well-defined, transparent, and consistent lines of responsibility. *What good judgement looks like to the PRA/FCA:* during a major operational change (a core banking migration, a material outsourcing, a business acquisition), the firm's governance arrangements remain adequate — the SMF4 knows which controls are operating degraded, has independent assurance that they are, and has a documented escalation plan if degradation exceeds tolerance. *Evidence:* project risk register with control-degradation tracking; weekly SMF4 risk briefings during the change period; board-level change-risk reporting; post-implementation review showing controls returned to full operation. *What failure looks like:* the TSB IT migration of 2018 — the CIO (subsequently fined) failed to have adequate oversight of outsourced programme delivery, did not act on warning signals, and did not escalate effectively; the FCA found this was a failure of governance adequacy under SYSC, not just a technical failure.
 
**B.4.3 MLR 2017 / JMLSG — Proportionate Risk-Based CDD.** *The obligation:* CDD measures must be proportionate to the risk (MLR 2017 Reg 28(12)); JMLSG methodology is persuasive but not mechanically prescriptive. *What good judgement looks like:* the firm can demonstrate why it assigned a given customer to a risk tier, with reference to its documented methodology; it can show that the methodology was reviewed when its customer base or product mix changed; it can show that high-risk customers received enhanced scrutiny and that the scrutiny produced a documented risk conclusion rather than just a process tick. *Evidence:* risk assessment for each material customer segment; case file for a PEP or high-risk customer showing the source-of-wealth assessment, the senior management approval, and the ongoing monitoring programme with evidence it ran. *What failure looks like:* NatWest's criminal conviction (2021) arose partly from the CDD framework failing to function in practice — customer risk grades were not updated as transaction patterns changed, and the escalation from transaction monitoring to SAR was systematically delayed; the firm had a documented framework but it did not operate as designed.
 
**B.4.4 COCON / SC1 — Effective Control of Firm Affairs.** *The obligation:* every SMF must take reasonable steps to ensure that the business falling within their area is controlled effectively (SC1). *What good judgement looks like:* the SMF identifies the key controls within their area, receives regular independent assurance that they operate effectively (not just attestations from the operational team), acts on adverse findings promptly, and documents the action. *Evidence:* SMF's own monitoring programme; escalation log; treatment of second-line findings; documented response to any internal audit report covering their area within 12 months. *What failure looks like:* Stuart Forsyth (Aviva Investors, 2021 settlement) — portfolio manager mispriced units; the SMF failed to have adequate oversight structures even after early warning signs were present; the regulator concluded that reasonable steps were not taken because the SMF's oversight was too distant from the operational level where the failure was occurring.
 
**B.4.5 DISP 1.4.1R — Impartial Complaints Investigation.** *The obligation:* investigate complaints competently, diligently and impartially. *What good judgement looks like:* the complaint handler has no commercial interest in the outcome; where the complaint concerns a product that the firm is actively selling, the investigation is separated from the front-book team; root cause analysis genuinely tests whether a systemic issue exists; redress is calculated on a basis that would make the customer whole, not on a basis that minimises payout. *Evidence:* complaints-handling governance showing separation from product; root-cause analysis documentation; redress calculation methodology; sample re-investigation by the compliance function. *What failure looks like:* FOS uphold rates above 60% in a product category are a signal of systematic impartiality failure; FCA uses FOS uphold data in supervisory assessments; firms with persistent high uphold rates face supervisory challenge even without a specific rule breach identified.
 
**B.4.6 PRA SS1/23 — Model Risk Management Judgement.** *The obligation:* apply a risk-tiering methodology to models based on materiality and complexity; conduct independent validation pre-deployment. *What good judgement looks like:* the firm identifies its IFRS 9 ECL model as Tier 1 (highest risk), validates it independently before each model version update, and monitors its performance against initial validation assumptions on a quarterly basis; where performance deteriorates, the firm escalates to the model risk committee and considers whether a model adjustment is required before the next accounting period close. *Evidence:* model inventory with risk tier and validation status; independent validation report with conclusions; performance-monitoring report with deviation analysis; model-risk committee minutes addressing material findings. *What failure looks like:* a model deployed with a "minor update" characterisation that bypasses the validation process; an IFRS 9 ECL model continuing in production despite known backtest deterioration; the PRA has cited examples in consultation feedback of firms treating SS1/23 as a documentation exercise without substance.

## SECTION C — SM&CR Mechanics in Operational Depth
 
### C.1 Pre-SMCR vs Post-SMCR — What Actually Changed
 
**The baseline before 2016.** Before SMCR came into force for banks in March 2016, the Approved Persons Regime (APR) existed but was widely regarded as ineffective. The APR required regulators to approve individuals for Controlled Functions (CFs) but enforced accountability at the firm level in most cases. Post-crisis enforcement action against individuals was slow and had a low success rate: in the aftermath of the 2008–2012 banking failures, the FSA brought fewer individual enforcement actions than Parliament expected. The Parliamentary Commission on Banking Standards (PCBS) in 2013 specifically identified that "the Approved Persons Regime has failed to provide individual accountability" and recommended what became SMCR.
 
**What changed in practice.** Board minutes: pre-SMCR board minutes at most banks recorded decisions collectively — "the Board resolved" — with minimal attribution to named executives. Post-SMCR, board minutes at well-governed banks explicitly attribute decisions, dissents, and information received to named SMFs; company secretaries were retrained on minute-taking standards. PRA Supervisory Statements (SS28/15 paragraph 6) make explicit that the Management Responsibilities Map and Statement of Responsibilities must be consistent with what is recorded in governance documents.
 
Escalation protocols: the threshold for written escalation dropped substantially. Senior executives who previously resolved matters over the phone or in corridor conversations now generate written decision records, particularly for matters that could fall within their prescribed responsibilities. Legal and Compliance teams reported (in anonymised post-implementation reviews) a significant increase in demand for written advice, partly because SMFs wanted documented evidence of having sought independent input before acting.
 
Legal / Compliance / Senior Management relationships: the relationship between SMFs and their internal counsel became more formal. Legal privilege became a more active consideration — SMFs' advice-seeking behaviour changed in ways that protect the quality of their evidence. Compliance teams gained leverage they did not previously have because their formal sign-off became part of the SMF's reasonable-steps evidence file.
 
**Where SMCR achieved its intent.** The FCA / PRA 2023 review of SMCR (CP23/20 discussion paper) found: (a) awareness of individual accountability is substantially higher than pre-SMCR; (b) governance documentation quality has improved; (c) in financial crime specifically, the MLRO role (SMF17) has clearer personal accountability than any predecessor arrangement; (d) the PRA's ability to take enforcement action against individuals has been enhanced — the evidential standard of "reasonable steps" is workable in enforcement compared to the APR's "knowingly concerned" standard.
 
**Where SMCR has not achieved its intent.** The same 2023 review found: (a) approximately 12% of firms were not using Statements of Responsibilities to accurately reflect operational responsibilities — SoRs had become boilerplate documents disconnected from how decisions were actually made; (b) in some firms, SMCR had produced defensive documentation without genuine accountability improvement — decisions are documented after the fact, not because the SMF has a genuine oversight process; (c) cultural change has been uneven — strong in financial crime and markets but weaker in consumer-facing product areas where accountability diffusion remained significant; (d) the Certification Regime has, in some firms, become an annual HR process rather than a genuine fitness assessment.
 
**An acknowledged limitation.** SMCR did not prevent the TSB IT failure (2018), the Wirecard collapse (2020, though Wirecard was not UK-regulated), or the various Consumer Duty breaches identified in 2023–2024. The regime creates accountability in retrospect; it does not guarantee foresight. The FCA itself acknowledged in CP23/20 that the regime could not be a substitute for good-quality risk management — it identifies accountability after failure, it does not guarantee that a capable person occupies the role before failure.

### C.2 The Reasonable Steps Doctrine — Operational Evidence Standard
 
**The legal standard.** FSMA s.66A(5): an SMF is not guilty of misconduct if they took such steps as a person in their position could reasonably be expected to take to avoid the contravention occurring or continuing. The burden is on the SMF to demonstrate that reasonable steps were taken; it is not for the regulator to demonstrate that they were not. This is a reversal of the evidential presumption relative to most civil liability frameworks.
 
**What the regulator has accepted as reasonable steps — from enforcement and supervisory statements.** (a) Evidence that the SMF had a structured information flow from the area of their responsibility, with documented receipt and review. The SMF4 (CRO) who receives a weekly KRI dashboard, signs it as reviewed, and escalates anomalies has evidence of active oversight — even if a failure subsequently occurs outside the indicators' coverage. (b) Independent assurance commissioned and acted upon. The SMF who commissions a compliance review of a function, receives the findings, and produces a documented response demonstrates a governance loop. (c) Delegation with oversight. An SMF who delegates day-to-day execution to appropriate persons (COCON SC3) while maintaining documented periodic oversight — defined frequency, defined scope — satisfies the reasonable-steps threshold where the delegation was appropriate and the oversight was exercised. (d) Seeking and acting on expert advice. An SMF who receives legal or regulatory advice before taking a material decision, documents the advice received, and can show it was acted upon, has evidence of due care. (e) Timely escalation of adverse information. The SMF who receives a concerning finding and escalates within a reasonable period to the Board or the appropriate regulator has taken a reasonable step; the SMF who sits on the finding does not.
 
**What has been rejected as insufficient — specific enforcement examples.** (a) "I was not told" as a defence where the SMF had sufficient information that a reasonable person in the role would have enquired further. In Carlos Abarca (TSB, December 2022), the CIO received programme-level risk reports; the FCA found that a competent CIO would have identified the warning signs in those reports and escalated or acted; not having done so was a failure of reasonable steps despite not having been explicitly informed of specific risks. (b) Relying entirely on management attestations without independent verification. An SMF who relies on their direct reports' assurances that controls are operating effectively, without any independent check, cannot rely on those attestations as reasonable-steps evidence if the controls are subsequently found to have failed. (c) Generic committee participation. Attendance at a committee is not equivalent to oversight. The FCA has required evidence that the SMF understood the material before them, engaged with it, and acted on findings — not merely that they were in the room. (d) SoR compliance at appointment without subsequent maintenance. An SMF whose SoR was accurate at appointment in 2020 but had not been updated following material changes to the firm's structure by 2023 cannot rely on the 2020 SoR to demonstrate current accountability boundaries.
 
**What a well-designed reasonable-steps evidence file looks like.** The following categories of evidence, maintained contemporaneously, constitute the core of a defensible file: (1) The information flow record: evidence of what information the SMF received, when, and in what format — calendar-tracked MI packs, dashboards with access logs, or meeting packs with read receipts. (2) The decision log: for material decisions within scope, a contemporaneous note recording the date, attendees, the question considered, the information reviewed, the options assessed, and the decision made. This is not a meeting minute — it is an SMF-specific record maintained by or for the SMF. (3) The oversight evidence: records of the SMF's periodic oversight activity — visits to the relevant function, QA sampling, 1:1 reviews with senior direct reports, and the specific questions asked. (4) The escalation log: evidence that adverse information was escalated appropriately — to the Board, to the risk committee, or to the regulator under SC4. (5) The delegation record: documentation of delegated responsibilities, the person they were delegated to, the basis for the delegation (appropriate person assessment), and the oversight mechanism. (6) Independent assurance outputs: reports from Compliance, Internal Audit, or external reviewers covering the SMF's area, with evidence of review and response.
 
**How this flows into RCM design.** A UK RCM requires columns not present in the US version: (a) SMF Accountable — the specific SMF holder accountable for this control under their SoR and prescribed responsibilities; (b) Reasonable Steps Evidence Type — what category of evidence the accountable SMF would need to demonstrate reasonable steps were taken in relation to this control; (c) Oversight Cadence — how frequently the SMF's oversight of this control is documented; (d) Delegation Chain — if the control is operated below the SMF level, the documented delegation pathway and oversight mechanism.

### C.3 SMF Role Boundary Mapping
 
The following covers eight material SMF functions for a mid-sized dual-regulated UK bank, in each case addressing prescribed responsibilities, typical firm-specific scope, process ownership, control accountability, and reasonable-steps evidence.
 
**SMF1 — Chief Executive Officer.** *Prescribed responsibilities (PRA/FCA):* overall responsibility for the firm's conduct and compliance; prescribed responsibility for financial resilience and recovery planning (PR A); responsibility for SMCR compliance within the firm (PR B); responsibility for the firm's intra-group management and reporting (PR F). *Typical firm-specific scope at a mid-sized bank:* all business lines; board and executive committee leadership; regulatory relationship with PRA and FCA CEO-to-CEO channels; firm-wide strategy and risk appetite. *Process categories typically owned:* no process category is "owned" by the CEO operationally, but the CEO is the escalation apex for any material risk event and is personally accountable for the firm meeting its regulatory obligations broadly. *Control types accountable for:* governance-level controls — board approval of risk appetite, ICAAP, ILAAP, OpRes self-assessment, Consumer Duty Annual Report; tone-from-the-top controls measured by employee surveys and supervisory assessments. *Reasonable steps evidence for SMF1:* board papers with SMF1 engagement evidenced; documented CEO actions in response to material risk events or supervisory findings; CEO risk committee reporting cadence; evidence of escalation to PRA/FCA where required by SC4.
 
**SMF2 — Chief Financial Officer.** *Prescribed responsibilities:* financial statements (PR C); capital adequacy (PR D); stress testing (PR L). *Typical scope:* financial reporting, ICAAP/ILAAP, regulatory capital and liquidity, treasury operations, planning and budgeting. *Process categories:* ICAAP/ILAAP process; financial close; regulatory reporting (COREP, FINREP); funds transfer pricing. *Controls accountable for:* financial reporting controls; capital adequacy controls; stress test model governance; regulatory reporting accuracy controls. *Reasonable steps evidence for SMF2:* ICAAP/ILAAP sign-off trail; regulatory reporting review cadence; capital management committee minutes with SMF2 engagement; model validation sign-off for financial models.
 
**SMF4 — Chief Risk Officer.** *Prescribed responsibilities:* production and integrity of the firm's risk framework (PR G); internal stress testing (PR M); OpRes (shared PR Z); financial crime controls where not separately designated. *Typical scope:* risk framework including credit, market, operational, model, and conduct risk; second-line oversight function; enterprise risk reporting; engagement with PRA on SREP and ICAAP outcomes. *Process categories:* risk appetite framework; ICAAP / ILAAP; model risk governance; second-line oversight of all material risk processes. *Controls accountable for:* second-line challenge controls; risk appetite breach escalation; KRI monitoring; RCM oversight — the CRO at most mid-sized banks is the second-line owner of the RCM framework itself. *Reasonable steps evidence for SMF4:* weekly KRI dashboard sign-off; risk committee engagement; second-line findings issued and tracked; PRA supervisory meeting preparation and follow-up documentation; SMF4-commissioned independent assurance against specific risk domains.
 
**SMF5 — Head of Internal Audit.** *Prescribed responsibilities:* responsibility for managing the internal audit function (PR I). *Typical scope:* audit plan (risk-based); audit execution; findings issuance; tracking remediation; reporting to audit committee; coordination with external auditor and PRA/FCA. *Process categories:* audit planning; audit execution; findings management; regulatory engagement on audit findings. *Controls accountable for:* third-line assurance controls; audit plan adequacy; independence safeguards. *Reasonable steps evidence for SMF5:* audit plan approved by audit committee; resource adequacy assessment; evidence of co-ordination with regulator on key findings; follow-up status on material open issues.
 
**SMF16 — Chief Compliance Officer / Head of Compliance Oversight.** *Prescribed responsibilities:* oversight of the firm's compliance with FCA rules (PR B, FCA-side); responsibility for whistleblowing (PR L); Consumer Duty oversight (as designated PR, 2023 addition). *Typical scope:* compliance framework; regulatory horizon scanning; consumer duty outcomes monitoring; complaints oversight; staff training; FCA regulatory relationship day-to-day. *Process categories:* compliance monitoring; complaints handling; Consumer Duty governance; training programme. *Controls accountable for:* compliance monitoring controls; FCA notification controls; Consumer Duty evidence controls. *Reasonable steps evidence for SMF16:* compliance monitoring plan with evidence of execution; FCA meeting preparation and outcomes; Consumer Duty board report; open findings tracker with remediation status.
 
**SMF17 — Money Laundering Reporting Officer.** *Prescribed responsibilities:* money laundering compliance and AML reporting (PR K). *Typical scope:* AML framework; SAR/DAML process; sanctions compliance; financial crime investigations; NCA relationship; MLRO Annual Report; financial crime training. *Process categories:* AML transaction monitoring and SAR process; sanctions screening; customer risk assessment; correspondent banking. *Controls accountable for:* SAR/DAML controls; sanctions screening controls; customer risk-grading controls; PEP management controls. *Reasonable steps evidence for SMF17:* MLRO Annual Report with board engagement evidence; SAR/DAML MI reviewed and signed off at frequency; financial crime control testing documented; NCA and OFSI engagement log. *Unique dimension:* the MLRO has a direct statutory relationship with the NCA via the DAML regime — this creates a personal accountability nexus that is operationally distinct from any other SMF role. When a DAML is submitted, the MLRO is personally attesting that suspicious activity has been identified and consent is sought; if the firm subsequently transacts within the moratorium period without obtaining consent, the MLRO bears personal exposure.
 
**SMF10 — Chair of Risk Committee.** *Prescribed responsibilities:* chairing the board risk committee (or sub-committee covering risk). *Typical scope:* oversight of the risk framework through the committee; challenge of risk appetite; scrutiny of CRO reports; oversight of ICAAP adequacy. *Controls accountable for:* risk committee governance controls; risk appetite oversight; escalation from committee to full board. *Reasonable steps evidence for SMF10:* risk committee terms of reference; minutes demonstrating substantive engagement; evidence of challenge and follow-up; independent advisors used.
 
**SMF11 — Chair of Audit Committee.** *Prescribed responsibilities:* chairing the board audit committee. *Typical scope:* oversight of internal audit; relationship with external auditor; financial reporting oversight; oversight of whistleblowing process. *Controls accountable for:* audit committee governance; external auditor independence; financial reporting oversight. *Reasonable steps evidence for SMF11:* audit committee terms of reference; private sessions with internal and external auditors; findings closure oversight; whistleblowing reports reviewed.

### C.4 The Certification Regime and Conduct Rule Monitoring
 
**The Certification Regime in operation.** The regime applies to "Certified Persons" — individuals performing Significant Harm Functions as defined in FCA Handbook SYSC 27 and the PRA equivalent. Material examples for a mid-sized bank include: material risk takers (as defined for remuneration purposes); client-dealing functions (customer-facing roles in regulated sales); algorithmic trading functions; proprietary traders; individuals managing regulated client assets; anyone with supervisory responsibility over the above. At a mid-sized UK bank, the certified population typically runs from a few hundred to several thousand individuals depending on business mix.
 
The regime requires firms to perform an annual fitness and propriety (F&P) assessment before certifying (or re-certifying) the individual. The F&P assessment covers: (a) honesty, integrity and reputation — typically including criminal records checks, regulatory references, credit checks where role-relevant, and internal conduct record review; (b) competence and capability — professional qualifications, training completion, and (for client-facing roles) competency assessment; (c) financial soundness — credit checks where relevant to the role. The firm must issue a formal certificate if the assessment is positive; the individual cannot perform the Significant Harm Function without a current certificate.
 
**Where the certification process typically breaks down.** (i) Regulatory references: the obligation to provide regulatory references to new employers (SYSC 22) is widely understood, but the quality of references received from previous employers varies significantly — some are technically compliant but operationally thin; (ii) the annual assessment becoming a calendared HR process rather than a genuine risk-proportionate review, with high-risk-population individuals receiving the same level of scrutiny as lower-risk populations; (iii) newly in-scope roles being missed because the HR system and the compliance system are not reconciled — when a business unit creates a new role, the compliance function does not always know that a Significant Harm Function has been established; (iv) certification lapsing during an individual's absence (maternity/paternity, long-term illness) without adequate management of their responsibilities.
 
**Conduct Rule monitoring — the operational process.** Conduct Rules apply to virtually all in-scope employees (COCON in FCA Handbook; PRA equivalent). The six Individual Conduct Rules and four Senior Manager Conduct Rules set behavioural standards. Firms are required to: (a) ensure all in-scope employees are trained on the Conduct Rules (including on joining and as rules change); (b) investigate potential Conduct Rule breaches; (c) notify the FCA (via Form REP008) of any Conduct Rule breach — annually in aggregate for most employees, promptly for SMFs and Certified Persons. The sixth Individual Conduct Rule — added in connection with Consumer Duty — requires staff to act to deliver good outcomes for retail customers, creating a Conduct Rule nexus for customer-facing compliance failures.
 
The monitoring infrastructure at most mid-sized banks spans: HR systems flagging disciplinary actions for compliance review; conduct breach investigation by Compliance; whistleblowing case management feeding into the breach-assessment process; surveillance outputs (where applicable) feeding into market conduct Conduct Rule assessment; and annual Conduct Rule attestation forms completed by all in-scope staff. The most common failure mode is a conduct breach being investigated and resolved by HR as a disciplinary matter without ever being assessed by Compliance for Conduct Rule breach significance and notified to the regulator.

## SECTION D — UK Process Architecture Research
 
### D.1 Anchor Process Selection — Validate and Finalise
 
The six anchor processes selected for the UK RCM are:
 
1. **Customer Onboarding — Retail and Corporate/SME** — Regulatory weight: MLR 2017 CDD (OBL-MLR-006 through 009), SYSC 6.3, Consumer Duty PRIN 2A (target market, communications, support). Instrumentability: high for digital retail onboarding (event logs from onboarding platform); moderate for corporate (EDD workflows partially manual, UBO assessments often off-system). Supervisory hot zone: financial crime systems and controls (B.2.3); Consumer Duty (B.2.1). Selected.
2. **Payments — CHAPS, Faster Payments, Cross-Border SWIFT** — Regulatory weight: PSRs 2017 (SCA, execution, unauthorised transactions), OBL-PSR-APP (fraud reimbursement), OBL-OFSI (sanctions screening), OBL-PSR2017-004 (CoP). Instrumentability: high for system-executed payments; moderate for override and exception handling which involves human judgement. Supervisory hot zone: APP fraud reimbursement (B.2.4); operational resilience (Payments is a near-universal IBS). Selected.
3. **AML Transaction Monitoring and SAR/DAML Process** — Regulatory weight: MLR 2017 ongoing monitoring (OBL-MLR-006), POCA 2002 via OBL-JMLSG-003, SYSC 6.3 (OBL-SYSC-004), MLRO Annual Report (OBL-SYSC-005). Instrumentability: moderate — monitoring engine produces alert events but investigation quality is human-dependent and largely unstructured. Supervisory hot zone: financial crime (B.2.3). Selected.
4. **Complaints and Consumer Duty Outcomes Handling** — Regulatory weight: DISP 1 (OBL-DISP-001 through 005), Consumer Duty PRIN 2A all four outcomes, FG21/1 (vulnerable customers). Instrumentability: moderate — complaint intake and SLA are trackable; root cause quality and vulnerable customer identification are human-dependent. Supervisory hot zone: Consumer Duty (B.2.1); vulnerable customers (B.2.6). Selected.
5. **Credit Underwriting, IFRS 9 Staging and Monitoring** — Regulatory weight: PRA SS1/23 (OBL-PRA-OP-005) for credit models and ECL, Consumer Duty PRIN 2A.3 (fair value in credit pricing), CONC for consumer credit. Instrumentability: high for quantitative model outputs; low for judgemental overlays and covenant review. Supervisory hot zone: model risk SS1/23 (B.2.5). Selected.
6. **Operational Resilience IBS Identification and Self-Assessment** — Selected over Capital Markets and SMCR Mapping as the sixth process. Rationale: the March 2025 compliance deadline makes this a live supervisory hot zone (B.2.2); the process is compliance-critical in a way that Capital Markets is not for most mid-sized banks focused on retail/commercial banking; the IBS Self-Assessment is a named FCA/PRA regulatory artefact with board sign-off requirement; the process is instrumentable from system telemetry (dependency mapping, incident logs). Capital Markets was excluded because it applies to a subset of mid-sized UK banks; SMCR Mapping was excluded as a process because it is better modelled as a governance dimension applied across all processes rather than a standalone process.

### D.2 Process Deep Dives
 
#### D.2.1 Customer Onboarding — Retail and Corporate/SME
 
**(a) What is actually moving through this process.** For retail: biometric identity capture (image and liveness check); extracted document data (passport MRZ or driving licence barcode); bureau data pull (Experian/Equifax/TransUnion); CIFAS fraud check; electoral roll cross-reference; initial AML/sanctions screening output; customer risk score (tier 1/2/3); product eligibility decision; consent and KYC data record created in core banking. For corporate/SME: company registration number and Companies House pull; PSC register extract; director identification documentation; UBO identification and verification analysis; source of wealth and source of funds documentation (for EDD-triggering cases); sanctions screening of directors, UBOs and linked entities; customer risk score; senior management approval record where EDD applies. Human decisions embedded at: EDD trigger decision (automated or human escalation); UBO identification for complex structures; PEP assessment; senior management approval for PEP and high-risk relationships.
 
**(b) System architecture.** Onboarding orchestration platform: Pega, Salesforce Financial Services Cloud, or bespoke. Identity verification vendor: Onfido, Mitek, Jumio (biometric liveness + document OCR). PEP/sanctions screening: Refinitiv World-Check, Dow Jones Risk & Compliance, or LexisNexis Bridger Insight. Credit bureau: Experian, Equifax, or TransUnion via API. CIFAS member fraud database: CIFAS API. Companies House: CH API (free, near-real-time). CRM: Salesforce, Microsoft Dynamics. Core banking customer master: Temenos T24/Transact, Finastra Fusion, FIS Profile. Document management: OpenText, SharePoint (for corporate file packs). Case management for EDD: ServiceNow, Archer, or standalone case tool. Hand-offs: between the automated onboarding front-end and the CDD case management tool (when EDD is triggered, the case is moved to a manual queue handled by analysts, often in an offshore captive); between the UK compliance sign-off and the core banking account creation system.
 
**(c) Where regulatory obligations embed.** OBL-MLR-006 (identity verification) fires at the first data-capture step — the onboarding platform calls the ID&V vendor; the outcome (pass/fail/refer) is a compliance event recorded in the CDD record. OBL-MLR-007 (UBO verification) fires at the corporate entity assessment step — the platform calls the Companies House API and displays PSC data; the analyst confirms UBO identity against the PSC record and uploads verification documentation. OBL-MLR-008 / OBL-MLR-009 (EDD/PEP) fires at the risk-scoring step — the screening tool returns a hit; the case is escalated; a senior manager approval is obtained and documented within the case file; this is the compliance moment that must be captured as a timestamped event. OBL-CD-001 (Consumer Duty overarching outcome) is embedded in the product suitability check — the onboarding system checks that the customer profile maps to the product's target market definition before the account is opened. OBL-GDPR-001 (lawful basis) fires at the data-collection step — the lawful basis record must be created in the customer data record at account opening.
 
**(d) Why it is hard to instrument for compliance.** The EDD and PEP investigation workflow is largely off-system for complex cases — analysts take the case file, open a separate spreadsheet or Word document, conduct their investigation, and populate the outcome back into the case management tool. The investigation trail (searches run, sources consulted, reasoning) exists only in the analyst's head and informal notes unless the firm has invested in a structured EDD workflow tool. Corporate UBO analysis for complex structures (tiered holding companies, LPs, trusts with nominee arrangements) involves external sources — Companies House, foreign registries, public records — that are manually searched; there is no system event log for this activity. The CoP check for the first payment after onboarding may not be linked back to the onboarding CDD record, creating a gap in the observable compliance chain.
 
**(e) Characteristic failure mode.** Customer risk grades assigned at onboarding and never updated as the customer relationship evolves. Ongoing monitoring — which is part of the same MLR obligation as initial CDD — operates on a calendar-driven schedule (annual or biennial review) that does not respond to changes in the customer's actual risk profile (new business activity, change of control, adverse media). Complex corporate structures onboarded with a nominal UBO identification that does not capture intermediate beneficial owner changes registered at Companies House months later. Document expiry — passports and driving licences used for identity verification expire; many banks do not have a systematic process to flag expiring verification documents until a significant time after the document has expired. The enforcement record (NatWest 2021, HSBC Continental Europe 2021) points to this ongoing-monitoring gap as the most persistent failure mode.
 
**(f) What a UK regulator asks to see.** In a financial crime supervision visit or s.166 covering onboarding: (i) the most recent FRA, version history, and board approval minute; (ii) the customer risk-grading methodology document and its JMLSG alignment; (iii) 10–20 specific corporate customer files selected by the regulator, including the CDD file, UBO certificate, risk-grade rationale, and any EDD documentation with senior management approvals; (iv) the onboarding QA programme — what percentage of cases are quality-reviewed, by whom, and what the finding rate is; (v) for PEP cases: the complete decision trail from screening hit to senior management approval; (vi) proof that CoP is integrated into the first outbound payment for new customers; (vii) the MLRO Annual Report covering the period, with board response; (viii) for offshore captive execution: the oversight framework, quality assurance sampling, and evidence that the UK MLRO has direct visibility of quality issues identified offshore.

#### D.2.2 Payments — CHAPS, Faster Payments, Cross-Border SWIFT
 
**(a) What is actually moving through this process.** Payment instruction data: originator account details, beneficiary name, beneficiary account (sort code/account number or IBAN/BIC), payment amount, payment reference, execution channel. For CHAPS: SWIFT MT103 or ISO 20022 pacs.008 message; settled via RTGS; pre-authorisation through CHAPS scheme rules. For Faster Payments (FPS): ISO 20022 pacs.002 message; real-time gross settlement during participating PSP operating hours; returns and recalls managed separately. For cross-border SWIFT: correspondent banking message chain; possible intermediary banks; currency conversion event; OFSI and OFAC screening chain across all correspondent nodes. At each stage: SCA authentication result, CoP match/close-match/no-match result, fraud risk score, sanctions screening output (possible hit/clear/refer), AML transaction monitoring event, execution confirmation, settlement message, reconciliation break event if applicable.
 
**(b) System architecture.** Payment hub: Finastra Fusion Payments Manager, Temenos Payments Hub, Volante VolPay, or proprietary. Sanctions screening: Fircosoft (part of SWIFT), Actimize AML Screening, or Oracle FCCM Sanctions Detection. AML transaction monitoring: Actimize AML Detect, NICE Actimize, SAS AML, Oracle FCCM. Fraud detection: Featurespace ARIC, BAE Systems NetReveal, Stripe Radar (for digital). CoP: access to the Pay.UK CoP service via a CoP scheme participant API or an intermediary provider such as Bottomline or Verizon Business. CHAPS connectivity: direct RTGS participant or through a settlement sponsor. FPS: direct participant or through a sponsored agent arrangement. Case management for payment holds: ServiceNow, Actimize Case Management. Reconciliation: Temenos Reconciliations, AutoRek. ISO 20022 migration: for CHAPS, the Bank of England completed the RTGS renewal migration to ISO 20022 messaging; SWIFT cross-border migration is in the 2023–2025 window.
 
**(c) Where regulatory obligations embed.** OBL-OFSI-001 (sanctions screening) fires before payment release — the payment instruction is passed to the screening engine; a hit triggers a hold and human review; clean transactions proceed. The compliance moment is the screening decision timestamp relative to the payment execution timestamp. OBL-PSR-APP-001 (APP fraud reimbursement) embeds at two points: the fraud risk score at payment initiation (which informs whether a warning is shown to the payer) and the CHAPS/FPS Consumer Vulnerability check (which determines if enhanced friction is appropriate). OBL-PSR2017-001 (SCA) fires at the authentication step — the bank must verify two independent factors before the payment is released; exemption logic must be documented and the decision to apply an exemption is a regulatory event. OBL-PSR2017-004 (CoP) fires at the beneficiary name entry step — the CoP service is queried; the result (match/close-match/no-match/unavailable) is presented to the customer; any override by the customer or the relationship manager is a compliance event.
 
**(d) Why it is hard to instrument for compliance.** Faster Payments executes in under two seconds end-to-end; compliance controls must operate within that window or hold the payment. Sanctions screening that takes longer than the FPS execution window creates a queue that must be managed — the payment clears while the screening is still running in some high-volume configurations. Sanctions list refreshes (OFSI publishes intra-day updates for Russia regime additions) create a window during which a payment might be screened against a stale list. Relationship manager CoP overrides — where a RM confirms to a customer that a mismatch is acceptable — are a compliance event that must be logged, but in many banks the override happens via a phone call that is not system-captured. Cross-border payments involving correspondent banks in multiple jurisdictions create a multi-screening chain where the UK firm can only control its own screening node.
 
**(e) Characteristic failure mode.** The canonical UK payments compliance failure at mid-sized banks is the CoP mismatch override. A relationship manager receives a call from a corporate client saying the payment is urgent and the name mismatch is a minor discrepancy. The RM verbally authorises the payment to proceed on the phone. The CoP override is not recorded in the payment system. The payment turns out to be an invoice fraud — a fraudster has substituted beneficiary account details on a genuine invoice. Under PSR PS23/3, the sending PSP bears 50% liability. The RM's override is discoverable only if the call was recorded; the firm has no system evidence of the deliberate decision. A second characteristic failure is the sanctions list timing gap: OFSI adds a Russian entity to the UK sanctions list at 2:00pm; the bank's batch-update process refreshes the list at 11:00pm; four CHAPS payments clear during the gap; the bank has a strict liability exposure under ECTEA 2022.
 
**(f) What a UK regulator asks to see.** In an OFSI or FCA financial crime visit: (i) the sanctions screening configuration — which list versions are in use, how frequently the list updates, whether intra-day updates are processed or batch; (ii) CoP override rate by channel and by business unit, with an explanation of the spike months; (iii) the escalation log for sanctions hits — the date of the hit, the disposition, and the time from hit to disposition; (iv) APP fraud reimbursement decisions for the last quarter, including the Consumer Standard of Caution application rate and the denial rationale; (v) FPS service availability statistics and any IBS impact tolerance breach events; (vi) the most recent ISO 20022 migration assurance report (for CHAPS participants); (vii) evidence that the firm's payment hold process can operate within the DAML moratorium — that a payment already cleared on suspicious grounds can be frozen pending NCA response within the 7-day window.

#### D.2.3 AML Transaction Monitoring and SAR/DAML Process
 
**(a) What is actually moving through this process.** Transactional data: account-level debit and credit history; transaction amounts, counterparties, narratives, channels, timing; behavioural baseline derived from historical peer-group analysis. Alert events: the monitoring engine compares transaction patterns against detection scenarios (rules, thresholds, or ML-based models) and generates alerts when thresholds are breached. Case records: an alert triggers a case record in the case management system; the analyst opens the case, reviews the underlying transactions and customer profile, and reaches a disposition. SAR record: where a case cannot be resolved as false positive, a SAR (Suspicious Activity Report) is drafted and submitted to the National Crime Agency via the NCA SAR Online portal. DAML record: where the suspicious activity involves a transaction the firm needs to conduct, a Defensive Against Money Laundering request is submitted; the 7 working day notice period and (if not refused) 31 calendar day moratorium are tracked. Customer communication restrictions: while a DAML is pending, the firm cannot "tip off" the customer that a SAR or DAML has been submitted (POCA 2002 s.333A); this creates an operational constraint on normal customer communication.
 
**(b) System architecture.** Transaction monitoring engine: Actimize AML Detect, NICE Actimize SAM, SAS AML, Oracle FCCM Transaction Monitoring, Quantexa (entity resolution and network analytics increasingly deployed). Customer data platform: core banking customer master (T24, Fusion, FIS Profile) plus a customer data warehouse feeding risk profiles. Case management: Actimize Case Management, NICE Actimize, Temenos Financial Crime Mitigation. SAR submission: NCA SAR Online portal (web-based; the NCA has been developing an API submission route but as of 2025 many firms still submit via the web portal). DAML tracking: typically manual or spreadsheet-based at smaller firms; integrated into Actimize or equivalent case tool at larger firms. Customer communication: contact centre and relationship management CRM, with tipping-off restrictions managed by manual hold flags.
 
**(c) Where regulatory obligations embed.** OBL-MLR-006 (ongoing monitoring) fires at the alert generation step — the monitoring engine identifies a potential suspicious pattern; this is the regulatory moment when the firm has "knowledge or suspicion" that triggers the SAR obligation timeline. The compliance critical window is from alert generation to SAR decision: the MLRO must assess whether the alert meets the reporting threshold; delay in this assessment increases POCA exposure. OBL-JMLSG-003 (SAR/DAML) fires when the investigation concludes that a report should be made — the NCA SAR Online submission timestamp is the compliance evidence. The DAML moratorium counter (7 working days from submission, up to 31 calendar days if consent is not given) is a time-critical compliance window; failure to track it correctly can result in a transaction proceeding outside the moratorium — a POCA offence. OBL-SYSC-005 (MLRO Annual Report) aggregates all monitoring and SAR activity into the annual governance artefact.
 
**(d) Why it is hard to instrument for compliance.** Alert disposition involves human judgement at the case analyst level; the decision to close as false positive versus escalate for SAR is subjective and the reasoning is often not captured in a structured format. Alert false-positive rates in UK retail banking typically run 85–97%; the investigation quality of the true-positive signal is therefore highly dependent on analyst skill and experience. The backlog problem: when alert volumes exceed analyst capacity (which happens during periods of market stress, customer base growth, or rule change), cases age; the oldest cases may have the most complex typologies requiring the most experienced analysts — but alert queue management typically prioritises volume throughput, not complexity. DAML moratorium tracking at many mid-sized banks is managed on a spreadsheet maintained by the MLRO team; there is no system-generated calendar event that enforces the moratorium deadline. The tipping-off restriction creates operational challenges when a DAML customer contacts the bank during the moratorium period for unrelated business — the front-line staff cannot see the tipping-off flag, do not know a DAML is pending, and may inadvertently disclose.
 
**(e) Characteristic failure mode.** The most documented UK failure mode (NatWest criminal conviction, 2021) is the confluence of: (i) transaction monitoring alerts aging in the queue without resolution; (ii) investigators closing complex cases as false positive because the queue pressure incentivises volume throughput; (iii) the escalation path from investigator to MLRO being informal and under-documented; (iv) the MLRO Annual Report not reflecting the true backlog position because MI is summarised. A second characteristic failure specific to UK banking: the DAML timing error. The firm identifies suspicious funds in an account. A DAML is submitted at 9am on Monday. The moratorium expires at 5pm on the seventh working day (following Monday if no bank holidays). At 4:30pm on the following Monday, a colleague in the payments team processes a customer-initiated withdrawal because the flag has not reached the payments system. The payment clears; POCA exposure materialises.
 
**(f) What a UK regulator asks to see.** In a financial crime supervision visit or s.166 covering AML monitoring: (i) the transaction monitoring rule/model inventory, including the last configuration review date, rationale for thresholds, and false-positive measurement methodology; (ii) alert backlog status — age distribution of open alerts, SLA compliance, and any periods where backlog exceeded a defined limit; (iii) SAR submission statistics — volume, typology breakdown, time from alert generation to SAR submission (the regulator looks for systematic delay); (iv) DAML register — all open and recent DAML submissions with moratorium tracking evidence; (v) a sample of 20–50 closed cases (selected by the regulator, not the firm) for re-investigation; (vi) the MLRO Annual Report with board-approved response; (vii) the QA programme for case investigation — what percentage are reviewed, by whom, and what findings the QA produced; (viii) evidence of SS1/23 model risk governance applied to the monitoring engine — validation status, performance monitoring, model risk tier.

#### D.2.4 Complaints and Consumer Duty Outcomes Handling
 
**(a) What is actually moving through this process.** Complaint record: expression of dissatisfaction received via any channel — telephony, branch, secure messaging, social media, intermediary, Claims Management Company (CMC). Complaint classification: product category, root cause (mapped to FCA taxonomy), vulnerability flag, channel. Investigation artefacts: account records, transaction data, product terms, call recordings, correspondence history, adviser records. Final response letter: the firm's reasoned determination, redress calculation (where applicable), and FOS referral information. Redress record: financial calculation and payment evidence. FCA Complaints Return data: aggregated data submitted via FCA Connect. Consumer Duty outcome data: customer satisfaction survey results, outcome KPIs (product performance, resolution rate, repeat contact rate), FVA data.
 
**(b) System architecture.** Complaint management system: Salesforce Service Cloud, Microsoft Dynamics Customer Service, or bespoke complaint platforms (Aptean, Respond, iCasework). Telephony and call recording: Genesys, Avaya, NICE CXOne — recordings are evidence in FOS proceedings. CRM: linked to complaint system for customer history. Redress calculation tool: typically spreadsheet-based for complex cases; automated for standard redress calculations (e.g., PPI had purpose-built tools). FCA Connect: browser-based submission portal for Complaints Return. Consumer Duty outcome data: typically populated from a combination of survey tools (Medallia, Qualtrics), operational dashboards (Power BI, Tableau), and product performance analytics.
 
**(c) Where regulatory obligations embed.** OBL-DISP-001 (complaint handling procedure) embeds at intake — every expression of dissatisfaction via any channel must be logged as a complaint; the firm cannot choose to handle some expressions informally and avoid the DISP clock. The FCA's definition of a "complaint" under DISP 2.3 is broad — it includes any oral or written expression of dissatisfaction that alleges the complainant has suffered or may suffer financial loss, material distress, or material inconvenience. OBL-DISP-003 (8-week hard deadline) fires at intake — the DISP clock starts immediately; the firm has 8 weeks to issue a final response regardless of complexity. OBL-CD-003 (foreseeable harm) embeds at the root cause analysis step — where a complaint identifies a product or process causing harm to the customer segment, the firm must assess whether foreseeable harm is occurring and take action beyond resolving the individual complaint. OBL-CD-008 (vulnerable customer support) embeds when the vulnerability flag is set — the complaint handling process must adjust (different communication format, extended response time if needed, different escalation path).
 
**(d) Why it is hard to instrument for compliance.** The 8-week DISP SLA is trackable; however, multi-channel complaint intake means that complaints received in branch or via third parties may be logged late, creating an inaccurate clock start. Vulnerability identification during complaints handling depends on the judgement of the complaint handler — there is no reliable system-based vulnerability detection unless the customer is already flagged in the CRM. Root cause analysis quality is subjective; the FCA's DISP reporting taxonomy provides categories, but firms classify complaints into categories inconsistently, making peer comparison and regulatory signal extraction difficult. The Consumer Duty outcomes monitoring obligation requires tracking customer outcomes over time — not just resolving individual complaints — which requires a data architecture that links complaint records, outcome survey data, and product performance data across a single customer journey view. Most mid-sized banks do not yet have this architecture fully operational.
 
**(e) Characteristic failure mode.** The DISP 8-week failure under volume stress. A large-scale product mis-selling allegation (or a regulatory change triggering retrospective complaints — as PPI demonstrated) overwhelms the complaint handling team. Cases approach the 8-week deadline without resolution; the firm sends extension letters that exceed the DISP framework; FOS receives a surge of referrals; the FOS uphold rate, which was previously manageable, spikes. The FCA uses FOS uphold rates in supervisory MI — a persistent above-peer uphold rate triggers supervisory attention even without a specific complaint trigger. A second characteristic failure: Consumer Duty Fair Value Assessment that identifies poor outcomes in a product segment but does not result in a product change. The FCA's July 2024 Dear CEO letter identified this specifically — firms producing FVAs that concluded "fair value" regardless of customer outcome data, without an action log demonstrating what changed as a result.
 
**(f) What a UK regulator asks to see.** In a Consumer Duty or DISP supervision visit: (i) complaints volume data by product, channel, and root cause for the prior 12 months with trend analysis; (ii) DISP SLA compliance — percentage resolved within 3 days, 8 weeks, and FOS escalation rate; (iii) FOS uphold rate by product category; (iv) vulnerable customer identification rate in complaints and evidence of adjusted handling; (v) root cause analysis outputs — which root causes are driving the most volume and what has been done about them; (vi) Consumer Duty Annual Report (board-approved) for the most recent year; (vii) Fair Value Assessment for one or two selected products — the methodology, the data, the conclusion, and the consequential action taken; (viii) outcome monitoring data — the firm's KPIs for each of the four Consumer Duty outcomes, measurement methodology, and trend over the prior 12 months; (ix) the Consumer Duty Board Champion's engagement log.

#### D.2.5 Credit Underwriting, IFRS 9 Staging, and Monitoring
 
**(a) What is actually moving through this process.** Application data: personal or company financial information, income or revenue verification (payslips, open banking, management accounts), credit bureau data, property valuation (for secured lending), purpose of credit. Underwriting decision: automated scorecard output, underwriter judgement overlay, pricing decision, terms and conditions. Loan record: booked exposure, original risk grade, covenant schedule (for commercial lending), IFRS 9 Stage at origination (Stage 1 for performing). Ongoing performance data: repayment history, arrears, delinquency events, updated bureau data, covenant test results, market value of collateral, IFRS 9 stage transitions (Stage 1 performing → Stage 2 significant increase in credit risk → Stage 3 credit-impaired). ECL calculation: probability of default (PD), loss given default (LGD), and exposure at default (EAD) inputs; macro-economic overlay; ECL provision output. Forbearance record: any modification of terms in response to customer financial difficulty.
 
**(b) System architecture.** Loan origination system (LOS): Temenos Originate, nCino, Salesforce Financial Services Cloud, or proprietary. Credit decisioning engine: FICO, Moody's Analytics (commercial), proprietary scorecards running in SAS or Python deployment. Open banking aggregation: Experian Open Banking, Moneyhub, Yapily (for affordability assessment). Credit bureau: Experian, TransUnion, Equifax (personal); Dun & Bradstreet, Creditsafe (commercial). Core banking (booking): Temenos T24, Finastra Fusion, FIS Profile. IFRS 9 ECL engine: Moody's Analytics ECL Engine, SAS Credit Risk, Zanders, Kamakura — running monthly close with auditable output. Collateral management: proprietary or specialist (NIKU, Finastra ICS). Covenant monitoring: typically spreadsheet-based or specialist (CreditSight, Covenant Review). Collections: Latitude by Genesys, FICO Debt Manager, or bespoke.
 
**(c) Where regulatory obligations embed.** OBL-PRA-OP-005 (SS1/23 model risk) fires at every model development, update, and deployment event in the credit process — the credit scorecard, the IFRS 9 PD model, the LGD model, the stress testing model, and (increasingly) the open banking affordability model must all pass through the model risk governance framework before deployment. The SS1/23 effective date of May 2024 means many models deployed before that date must now be retro-assessed against the new standard. OBL-CD-006 (fair value assessment for credit products) fires at the annual product review — the firm must assess whether the pricing of each credit product represents fair value to the target market, considering the annual percentage rate, the fee structure, and the distribution of outcomes across the customer population. OBL-CONC 5 (creditworthiness assessment) fires at origination — the firm must assess the customer's ability to repay the credit in a way that is not "unduly burdensome"; this assessment must be evidenced per application.
 
**(d) Why it is hard to instrument for compliance.** IFRS 9 Stage 2 transition is a model-driven decision with a judgemental overlay. The "significant increase in credit risk" (SICR) trigger is defined by the firm's methodology; different methodology choices produce materially different Stage 2 populations. The PRA and external auditors review SICR methodology and override rates. Management overlays — where the model's Stage output is overridden upward or downward by the credit team — are a frequent source of examination; override documentation is often thin. Covenant monitoring for commercial lending is a calendar-driven manual process: the borrower submits quarterly financial statements; a covenant analyst reviews them against the test definitions; a breach (or waiver request) triggers a workflow. Where the covenant monitoring process is managed in spreadsheets, the evidence trail is incomplete. Forbearance identification is inconsistent: customers in genuine financial difficulty may be offered payment deferrals or interest-rate reductions without being formally classified as forborne, understating the distressed population and potentially understating ECL provisions.
 
**(e) Characteristic failure mode.** The characteristic failure in UK credit compliance is the "zombie covenant" — a covenant condition embedded in a commercial loan agreement at origination but never connected to the monitoring system. The borrower's financial deterioration goes unobserved because the covenant test is never actually run. This is particularly common in loans originated before the current loan management system was implemented, where covenant terms were not migrated from legacy documentation into the current monitoring workflow. A second characteristic failure is IFRS 9 Stage 2 migration timing: a borrower's credit quality deteriorates over six months; the IFRS 9 model flags a SICR at month four; the model output is overridden by the relationship manager on the basis of a verbal assurance from the borrower; at month nine, the borrower defaults; the ECL catch-up provision is material; the PRA questions why the override was maintained without documented evidence.
 
**(f) What a UK regulator asks to see.** In a PRA SS1/23 or ECL supervision visit: (i) the model inventory covering all credit models — scorecard, PD, LGD, EAD, IFRS 9 macro-overlay — with validation status and performance monitoring results; (ii) IFRS 9 Stage migration analysis — the population moving between stages each month, the override rate, the override approval documentation; (iii) backtesting results for the PD model — predicted default rate vs. actual default rate over trailing 12–24 months; (iv) forbearance identification and monitoring data — the population, the ageing, and the ECL treatment; (v) covenant monitoring evidence for a sample of 10–15 commercial loans — the test results, any breach flags, and the response; (vi) Consumer Duty Fair Value Assessment for the firm's primary consumer credit products; (vii) creditworthiness assessment process documentation and a sample of individual application assessments; (viii) SS1/23 model risk governance documentation — the framework, the tiering methodology, the validation function's independence evidence.

#### D.2.6 Operational Resilience IBS Identification and Self-Assessment
 
**(a) What is actually moving through this process.** IBS register: a document listing each Important Business Service, its owner (SMF), its impact tolerance (maximum disruption time), and its assessment status. Dependency mapping artefacts: the mapping of each IBS to its supporting people, processes, technology systems, facilities, data, and third-party providers. Scenario test design documents: the specification of each severe-but-plausible disruption scenario, the scope of the test, the assumptions, and the methodology. Scenario test results: evidence of whether the firm can remain within impact tolerance; identified gaps; remediation actions. Self-Assessment document: the annual board-approved document submitted to regulators on request, covering IBS identification, tolerances, mapping, testing, and remediation. Material outsourcing register: the listing of all material outsourcing arrangements per SS2/21. CTP dependency register: where CTPs have been designated, the firm's mapping of its reliance on those providers.
 
**(b) System architecture.** GRC platform: ServiceNow GRC, Archer, MetricStream — used to maintain the IBS register and dependency mapping in a structured, version-controlled environment. Process mapping tools: Visio, Lucidchart, or GRC-embedded process modelling — used to create visual dependency maps. Business continuity management system: SAI360, Fusion Framework, or equivalent — used to manage recovery plans and scenario test evidence. CMDB (Configuration Management Database): ServiceNow CMDB, BMC Helix — used as the technology dependency source of truth; the most current CMDBs feed IBS mapping in near-real-time. Third-party risk management platform: LogicGate, ProcessUnity, OneTrust — used to maintain outsourcing register and third-party assessments. Incident management: PagerDuty, ServiceNow Incident Management — generates the incident timeline evidence used in scenario tests and real incidents. Cybersecurity tooling: SIEM (Splunk, Microsoft Sentinel), vulnerability management — feeds into cyber scenario test design.
 
**(c) Where regulatory obligations embed.** OBL-OR-001 (IBS identification) fires at the annual review cycle and at any material business change — when a new product is launched, a new outsourcing arrangement is entered, or a significant technology change occurs, the IBS register must be reviewed to determine whether a new IBS has been created or an existing IBS affected. OBL-OR-003 (dependency mapping) is an ongoing obligation — the mapping must reflect current state, not the state at the last annual review; material changes to technology, people, or outsourcing trigger a mapping update requirement. OBL-OR-004 (scenario testing) fires annually at minimum, and when a new scenario is identified as materially different from tested scenarios. OBL-OR-005 (Self-Assessment) fires annually — the board must approve the document before the financial year end. OBL-OR-006 (material outsourcing) fires before a new material outsourcing arrangement is entered — the PRA must be notified in advance; the contract must contain prescribed terms.
 
**(d) Why it is hard to instrument for compliance.** The core instrumentation challenge is mapping currency. The IBS register is a point-in-time document; the technology and people landscape changes continuously. A CMDB can provide a near-real-time view of technology dependencies — but CMDBs are notoriously difficult to keep current; at many UK banks, the CMDB has a staleness rate of 15–30% for non-critical assets. When an infrastructure team decommissions a legacy server and replaces it with a cloud service, the CMDB update depends on an IT change management process that does not always integrate with the OpRes mapping tool. Scenario testing is expensive — mobilising a cross-functional test team for a half-day or full-day exercise has direct cost; the temptation is to design narrow tests that can be completed quickly rather than comprehensive tests that genuinely probe tolerance boundaries. Cloud concentration is difficult to quantify: many banks' IBS dependencies converge on the same cloud provider for hosting, backup, and SaaS applications; the aggregated dependency on a single cloud provider may be material but is rarely surfaced in a single view.
 
**(e) Characteristic failure mode.** The stale-mapping scenario test. The firm's IBS mapping, produced in January 2024, references a specific data centre as the primary hosting location for the payments IBS. In March 2024, the firm migrated the payments infrastructure to AWS as part of a cloud transition programme. The IBS mapping was not updated. In the annual scenario test in October 2024, the cyber disruption scenario is designed around the January 2024 data centre architecture. The test passes — but it is testing the wrong architecture. The regulator asks to see the scenario test; a competent supervisor identifies the mismatch between the scenario and the current dependency map. The Self-Assessment is now inaccurate. The firm must remediate before re-submission. A second characteristic failure: the third-party concentration blind spot. A mid-sized bank uses AWS for its core banking application, its payments hub, its data warehouse, and its document management system. Each outsourcing arrangement has been assessed and registered individually. No single view shows that AWS disruption would simultaneously affect four IBSs. The concentration risk is invisible until a real event occurs.
 
**(f) What a UK regulator asks to see.** In an OpRes supervision visit post-March 2025: (i) the IBS register with owner allocation, impact tolerances, and review date; (ii) the most recent scenario test evidence for each IBS — design document, test execution evidence, results, and gap closure; (iii) the mapping artefacts for two or three IBSs — the regulator typically asks for the payment IBS and a customer-facing IBS; (iv) evidence that the board approved the Self-Assessment — board minute or signed approval page; (v) the material outsourcing register — completeness, and the PRA notification log for new material arrangements entered in the last 12 months; (vi) CTP dependency mapping (if CTPs have been designated by the time of the visit); (vii) remediation plan for any tolerance breaches identified in scenario testing; (viii) evidence that the OpRes PR holder (the SMF with the OpRes Prescribed Responsibility) is engaged with the process — their oversight evidence, decision log, and the escalation pathway for tolerance breaches.

## SECTION E — UK Stakeholder Reality
 
### E.1 CRO / SMF4 — Narrative
 
**Mental map.** The UK CRO operates simultaneously across two mental frameworks: the enterprise risk taxonomy (credit, market, operational, model, conduct, cyber, third-party, climate, financial crime, liquidity) and the SMCR accountability architecture (which risks fall within their Prescribed Responsibilities, which SMFs they must influence without direct authority, where their "reasonable steps" boundary sits). The unit of analysis is the risk profile of the firm as it sits at this moment relative to appetite — but the UK CRO frames this not only as an institutional assessment but as a personal accountability question. The PRA's SREP, the ICAAP/ILAAP cycle, the board risk committee pack — all of these are public documents in the sense that the PRA has read them and formed a view; any material inconsistency between what is documented and what is happening operationally is a personal reasonable-steps problem.
 
**Decision rhythm.** The PRA Periodic Summary Meeting (PSM) is the anchor event in the supervisory calendar — typically annual for a Cat 3 firm, more frequent for Cat 2. The CRO is the lead executive in PSM preparation. The preparation cycle begins 6–8 weeks before the meeting: the supervisory request list (what the PRA has asked for) is mapped against available evidence; gaps are identified; the CRO decides which gaps to acknowledge and which to remediate before the meeting. Post-meeting, the supervisory feedback (a written summary or RMP) drives the remediation calendar. Internally: monthly Board Risk Committee (BRC), weekly ExCo risk forum, daily exception reports. The ICAAP cycle adds an annual burst of intensive work — typically 3–4 months of development, stress testing, and board review.
 
**Genuine fears.** (1) A PRA-imposed Risk Mitigation Programme (RMP) naming the CRO's function and requiring demonstrable remediation within a timeframe that is publicly discoverable — even where there is no public Final Notice, the RMP exists on the CRO's supervisory file. (2) A Skilled Persons Review (s.166) triggered by the PRA concluding that the firm's ICAAP or risk framework is inadequate — the CRO is the primary evidence witness and the primary target for findings. (3) SMF4 personal enforcement action following a risk event where the PRA concludes that reasonable steps under the SoR were not taken — the investigation begins with the CRO's own evidence file. (4) A PRA supervisor forming the view in a PSM that the CRO does not have adequate visibility of what is happening in their risk domains — this is not a formal finding but it shapes the PRA's supervisory posture for the following 12 months. (5) A board paper that understated risk being cited post-event in an enforcement action as evidence that the CRO was not candid with the board.
 
**Information diet.** The CRO consumes a weekly integrated risk dashboard (compiled from second-line challenge outputs, KRI monitoring, and incident reports). This is supplemented by a daily exception report flagging any breach of risk appetite, any material loss event, any regulatory notification, and any operational incident touching an IBS. Externally: PRA Annual Report, the Bank of England Financial Stability Report (two per year), PRA supervisory statements (any new SS is read within a week of publication), FCA Dear CEO letters in the CRO's risk domains, peer bank enforcement actions (read via legal advisory newsletters the same day they are published). The format preference is structured and brief — the CRO does not want narrative unless the narrative is consequential; the dashboard must be actionable, not descriptive. The board pack must be defensible in front of a PRA supervisor who will read it with scepticism.
 
**Adjacent friction.** With the CFO: appetite-versus-RWA tension — credit appetite that generates sustainable returns without breaching capital ratios; the PRA SREP Pillar 2A add-on depends on risk profile which the CRO cannot independently control. With the CEO: risk appetite challenge — the CEO faces commercial pressure to lend, launch, or enter new markets; the CRO must say no while maintaining the relationship. With the Head of Compliance (SMF16): territory disputes on conduct risk, model risk, and Consumer Duty — both functions have legitimate claims on some of the same risk domains. With Internal Audit (SMF5): findings resolution — when Internal Audit produces a finding in the CRO's domain, the CRO is accountable for remediation but may dispute the finding's characterisation.
 
**Product tolerance limits.** The UK CRO will not use a risk platform whose outputs cannot be mapped to PRA reporting categories (COREP, risk appetite statement, ICAAP) — the tool must speak the regulator's language. Will not adopt AI recommendations for risk appetite decisions without full model explainability documentation that can withstand a PRA challenge. Will not use any tool that creates a discoverable record of risk intelligence not acted upon — the platform must support action, not generate evidence of inaction.
 
**UK-specific dimension.** The PRA's individual-firm supervisory intensity (SREP, PSM, ICAAP challenge) creates an accountability pressure with no US equivalent at this intensity for mid-sized firms. The "reasonable steps" requirement means the CRO must personally maintain an evidence architecture — not just receive reports but demonstrate, with a paper trail, that they reviewed and acted on them. The dual-regulator structure means the CRO manages two supervisory calendars, two evidence demands, and occasionally two conflicting supervisory views on the same risk.

### E.2 Head of Compliance (SMF16) and Head of Financial Crime (SMF17) — Narrative
 
**Mental map.** SMF16 operates across an obligation coverage matrix: every FCA obligation mapped to a control, every control tested, every gap either remediated or accepted with senior management sign-off. The Consumer Duty shift (post-July 2023) has moved SMF16 away from a pure rule-compliance paradigm toward an outcomes-monitoring function — the question is no longer "did we follow the right process?" but "did the customer receive a good outcome?" This requires a fundamentally different evidence architecture. SMF17 thinks in cycles: the financial crime risk assessment cycle, the SAR submission cycle, the MLRO Annual Report cycle, and the regulatory relationship cycle with the NCA (DAML submissions) and OFSI. The unit of analysis for SMF17 is the individual case — not the population — because personal accountability attaches to decisions about individual customers in a way that is unique to the MLRO role.
 
**Decision rhythm.** SMF16: monthly compliance committee; quarterly Consumer Duty outcomes review; FCA supervisory meeting cadence (typically annual or event-driven for FCA); DISP 6-monthly Complaints Return submission cycle; annual Consumer Duty board report. SMF17: daily monitoring of DAML register (moratorium deadlines are not forgiving); weekly SAR MI review; monthly financial crime governance committee; MLRO Annual Report (typically produced Q1 for prior year); ad hoc OFSI engagement on frozen asset matters; FCA supervision of financial crime (typically thematic or event-triggered rather than formal annual cycle for mid-sized banks, but NCA engagement is more frequent).
 
**Genuine fears.** SMF16: (1) A Consumer Duty thematic review naming the firm as exhibiting patterns of poor outcomes — the FCA publishes firm-named supervisory actions; (2) a FOS uphold rate spike in a product category triggering FCA supervisory attention before the firm has identified the root cause; (3) a s.166 in the conduct space where the skilled person identifies Consumer Duty evidence architecture gaps; (4) a Dear CEO letter — a public signal of supervisory concern that hits firm reputation and, increasingly, SMF16's own career. SMF17: (1) An OFSI strict-liability breach under ECTEA 2022 for a sanctions payment that cleared during a list refresh window; (2) a criminal conviction risk for the firm under POCA arising from a DAML submission error or moratorium failure — the MLRO could face personal liability; (3) a NatWest-style criminal enforcement action; (4) the FCA identifying in a financial crime supervision visit that the MLRO Annual Report understated the true state of the financial crime framework; (5) a SAR backlog that becomes a regulatory finding while the MLRO has been reporting manageable performance.
 
**Information diet.** SMF16 monitors: complaints MI weekly; Consumer Duty outcome KPIs monthly; compliance monitoring plan execution quarterly; FCA correspondence and Dear CEO letters immediately; FOS uphold data each publication cycle. SMF17 monitors: DAML register daily; SAR submission MI weekly; financial crime KRIs (alert volumes, false-positive rate, SAR conversion rate) weekly; OFSI list updates (particularly Russia regime additions) on day of publication; NCA SAR Annual Report and SARs in Action bulletin publications immediately on release.
 
**Adjacent friction.** SMF16 and SMF4 (CRO): Consumer Duty has expanded SMF16's territory into what was previously purely a risk function — the CRO and SMF16 both claim ownership of conduct risk and model risk (for credit decisioning). SMF17 and operations (payments, onboarding): the MLRO needs transaction holds, customer account freezes, and data access that operations process owners experience as friction to throughput and customer experience. SMF16 and product: Consumer Duty's fair value requirement creates tension with product teams whose pricing models were optimised under a different regulatory regime. The standing argument that never resolves: whether an MLRO should also own the Sanctions function or whether Sanctions should sit with the CRO — governance models vary and neither is clearly right in regulation; the argument resurfaces every time OFSI enforcement creates confusion about ownership.
 
**UK-specific dimension.** The direct FCA relationship that SMF16 carries is personal in a way the US compliance function head does not experience — the FCA supervisor knows the SMF16 by name and will ask them directly why they did or did not take a particular action. The DAML regime creates a personal NCA relationship for SMF17 that is operationally unique — no US equivalent exists. Consumer Duty has structurally expanded SMF16's responsibility from rule compliance to outcomes evidence; this is not a process change, it is a function redesign requiring different skills, data architecture, and governance.

### E.3 Compliance Officers and Audit Managers — Narrative
 
**Mental map.** The testing professional's mental model is sample-based and evidence-first. The world consists of populations (of controls, transactions, decisions) and samples; testing is the practice of inferring whether the population meets the regulatory standard from the sample. The evidence architecture is the foundation: where does proof of control operation live, how is it extracted, what does it look like, and can it be reproduced. The s.166 preparation experience has shaped the modern UK compliance tester's approach — a s.166 review is operationally the highest-stakes evidence request the function faces, and the practitioner knows that every file they maintain might be reviewed by a skilled person who is specifically looking for gaps.
 
**Decision rhythm.** Testing plan governed by the annual compliance monitoring plan (for compliance officers) or the risk-based audit plan approved by the Audit Committee (for audit managers). FCA/PRA information requests (s.165 requests) arrive with typical turnaround times of 5–15 business days depending on scope — these can arrive at any time and take priority over the scheduled plan. Consumer Duty outcomes monitoring has created a new quarterly reporting cycle that did not previously exist. s.166 mobilisation is an event-driven response: when the requirement notice arrives (or the voluntary review begins), the testing and evidence function is activated on an intensive project basis.
 
**Genuine fears.** Compliance officer: (1) the Friday afternoon s.165 request with a Tuesday deadline — having to locate evidence that was known to exist somewhere but whose exact location is unclear; (2) a finding in their testing programme that the SMF16 must escalate to the FCA — the quality of the testing evidence determines whether the escalation is manageable or embarrassing; (3) a s.166 skilled person reviewing their workpapers and finding methodology gaps. Audit manager: (1) a finding reported to the Audit Committee that the first-line challenges as factually wrong — requiring a re-investigation while the finding is live in the register; (2) an external auditor or PRA reviewing an audit report and asking why a finding was not made in an area that subsequently produced an event; (3) an Internal Audit report that the PRA treats as a benchmark against which the firm's self-reported performance is tested.
 
**Information diet.** Testing-focused: internal control test results, evidence samples, exception rates; open issue registers; regulatory obligation register updates; FCA/PRA thematic reviews and published s.166 outputs (where available). External: FCA regulatory updates, JMLSG revisions, professional body publications (IIA, ACCA, CIIA); FOS decisions on products in scope of testing programme. Format preference: structured workpaper templates; population-level data access; searchable document repositories with meaningful version control.
 
**Adjacent friction.** With the first-line process owner: evidence retrieval — the testing function asks for transaction-level evidence; the process owner's team regards evidence requests as a distraction from operational throughput. This argument is structural (the throughput owner and the testing function have incompatible cadences) and recurs on every test cycle. With senior management: finding severity calibration — the testing function may rate a finding as material; the business line head wants it rated lower; the compliance function must hold the line while managing the relationship. Post-Consumer Duty, a new friction has emerged: the outcomes-monitoring obligation requires data from multiple operational functions (call centre, product, servicing) that do not naturally flow to compliance; data sharing is contested.
 
**Product tolerance limits.** Will not adopt population-testing tools that do not produce a documented, defensible audit trail back to source data — the workpaper must be reproducible. Will not use AI-assisted finding identification without the ability to interrogate and challenge the model's reasoning — every finding must be defensible in front of a regulator or skilled person. Will not accept automated evidence collection that does not distinguish between the evidence as it existed at the control operation date versus the evidence as it exists when retrieved — timestamp integrity is non-negotiable.
 
**UK-specific dimension.** The s.166 preparation experience is genuinely different from US exam preparation. In a US exam, the firm prepares a structured evidence pack and guides the examiner through it; in a s.166, the skilled person designs their own evidence request list and the firm provides whatever is asked for, regardless of preparedness. This reverses the information asymmetry. Post-Consumer Duty, outcomes monitoring has created a testing discipline that does not fit neatly into the traditional "test a specific control" workpaper — the tester must sample outcomes rather than control operations, which requires different methodology and different evidence standards.

### E.4 Process Owners and Operations Leads — Narrative
 
**Mental map.** The process owner thinks in terms of throughput, SLA performance, unit cost, and customer satisfaction — these are the metrics on which they are measured and rewarded. The compliance obligation is experienced primarily as a friction applied to throughput. Consumer Duty has changed this calculation for UK process owners in a way that has no US equivalent: the Consumer Support Outcome (PRIN 2A.6) means that the call centre, the servicing team, and the onboarding team are now compliance evidence sources — their performance data (hold times, resolution rates, callback fulfilment, vulnerable customer identification rates) is regulatory evidence. This has brought process owners into the compliance data architecture in a way that previously applied only to compliance professionals.
 
**Decision rhythm.** Daily operations huddle: queue health, exception ageing, overnight failures, staffing. Weekly performance review: SLA by process, exception rates, quality scores. Monthly Ops Council: financial performance, headcount, technology requests. Quarterly Business Review: strategic performance, Consumer Duty outcome data review, APP fraud reimbursement P&L attribution.
 
**Genuine fears.** (1) A Faster Payments service outage during peak payment window — IBS disruption event, regulator notification required, customer harm materialising in real time and measured against impact tolerance. (2) Consumer Duty data showing that their process is producing customer harm at scale — the process owner now owns the Consumer Duty evidence for their process and cannot delegate it to compliance. (3) An APP fraud reimbursement decision their team got wrong — PSR escalation, financial penalty, and public scrutiny of the decision rationale. (4) An offshore captive team data quality problem surfacing in an FCA review — the process owner is accountable for the quality of work executed offshore under UK SMCR governance even though the work is physically executed in India, the Philippines, or South Africa.
 
**Adjacent friction.** With Compliance: the standing argument about control burden versus throughput. Every additional control step in the process adds time; the process owner experiences this as cost and customer experience degradation. The compliance function does not experience this cost directly. This is the most persistent friction in UK banking operations and no organisational design has resolved it. With Technology: system performance versus operational stability — the process owner wants system changes deployed quickly; the technology function imposes change-management gates that delay. With the offshore captive: geographical and cultural distance creates oversight friction — quality assurance data from the India captive reaches the UK process owner with a lag, and the corrective action cycle is slow.
 
**UK-specific dimension.** APP fraud reimbursement has made fraud outcomes a direct P&L item for payment operations — this is structurally new as of October 2024 and has changed how payment operations leadership is incentivised. Consumer Duty has expanded the definition of what "good operations" means — it is no longer sufficient to meet the SLA; the process must now demonstrably deliver a good outcome for the customer in a way that is documented and regulatory-grade. The UK offshore captive model (extensively used by Barclays, HSBC, Lloyds, NatWest) creates a structural control perimeter challenge: UK SMCR accountability sits onshore, execution sits offshore, and the evidence chain must bridge the two.

### E.5 Frontline Process Executives — Narrative
 
**Mental map.** The frontline executive's cognitive model is case-and-queue-based. A case is the unit of work. The queue is the environment in which cases are managed. Decision-making is guided by the screen — what the system presents, what the SOP says, and what peer practice has established. For a KYC analyst in a UK bank's Hyderabad captive, the regulatory framework governing their decisions is UK law (MLR 2017, POCA 2002), the investigation standard is set by JMLSG and the bank's own procedures, and the governance accountability chain runs up to the UK MLRO. The analyst may not know the specific UK statutory provisions, but they operate under them.
 
**Decision rhythm.** Case allocation at shift start. Decision cadence: a KYC analyst at a mid-sized UK bank processes 6–12 corporate CDD cases per day or 25–40 retail CDD cases per day depending on complexity tier. A SAR investigator handles 3–8 cases per day. Each case involves a structured sequence: open case → review data → run searches → apply policy → reach disposition → document → close. Escalation to the UK supervisor or MLRO team triggers when a case is outside the analyst's decision authority (EDD cases, PEP cases, DAML-eligible cases).
 
**Genuine fears.** For a certified frontline executive: (1) misclassifying a case — closing a genuine suspicious activity alert as a false positive, or approving an EDD case that should have been escalated — constitutes a potential Conduct Rule breach under IC2 (due care, skill and diligence) or IC4 (due regard to customers); (2) handling a vulnerable customer case incorrectly — the firm's FG21/1 guidance requires the analyst to identify vulnerability signals and adapt; missing this has both regulatory and consumer consequences; (3) for an analyst in the offshore captive, the sense of distance from the regulatory consequences can create detachment — but the Conduct Rules (post-SMCR expansion to offshore captive staff executing UK-regulated functions) mean that personal accountability exists regardless of geography.
 
**Information diet.** On-screen guidance embedded in the case tool. SOP accessible via the intranet. Peer consultation via instant messaging (the most widely used form of real-time decision support, largely outside the documented governance framework). Supervisor presence — for escalations and for complex case support. Training: AML certification (ICA or equivalent, mandated annually or biennially at most UK banks); Conduct Rules awareness training; Consumer Duty training for customer-facing staff. The gap between what the training covers and what the daily case load requires is the primary knowledge deficit — the SOP answers generic cases; the analyst's daily cases include edge cases the SOP does not cover.
 
**Adjacent friction.** With supervisors: target tension — the analyst's throughput target may create implicit pressure to close cases quickly; the supervisor's QA function checks the quality of those closures; the two incentives are in tension. With UK oversight team: cultural and communication distance in the offshore context — the UK oversight function issues instructions; the offshore team executes; feedback on edge cases takes 24–48 hours across time zones; the analyst makes a judgment call in the meantime.
 
**UK-specific dimension.** The extension of Conduct Rules to offshore captive staff executing UK-regulated processes is a UK regulatory feature that practitioners in the US and Indian frameworks do not face in the same form. An analyst in Hyderabad closing a SAR case for a UK bank is operating under COCON — their decision is subject to UK Conduct Rule standards and their employer is required to have a process to identify Conduct Rule breaches and notify the FCA. The Consumer Duty sixth Conduct Rule (deliver good outcomes for retail customers) applies to frontline staff at the point of customer interaction — this is not an abstract governance obligation but an operational one that manifests in case handling decisions.

## SECTION F — UK Structural Frictions
 
### F.1 The Four-Language Friction
 
**Friction name and description.** UK banking operates with four functionally distinct vocabularies in simultaneous use: (1) Regulatory language — the FCA Handbook and PRA Rulebook's obligation framing ("SYSC 6.3.6R requires..."; "Consumer Duty Outcome 3 means..."); (2) Risk taxonomy language — the CRO's enterprise risk framework ("operational risk event category code OR-003"; "model risk tier 2 finding"); (3) Process language — the operations function's workflow terminology ("case closed," "SLA met," "exception aged"); (4) Personal accountability language — the SMCR framework's attribution framing ("SMF4 reasonable steps evidenced by..."; "PR K obligation met through..."). The same event — a transaction monitoring alert that produces a SAR — generates four simultaneously active descriptions. None of them translates cleanly into the others.
 
**Why it is structural — tests passed.** (1) Incompatible incentives: the risk function is rewarded for risk precision, operations for throughput, SMCR-aware roles for personal defensibility — none of these incentives rewards cross-language translation. (2) Regulatory inscription: the four languages are each mandated by separate regulatory instruments (SYSC/MLR for obligation language; PRA ERMF expectations for risk taxonomy; operational SLAs for process language; SMCR FSMA for accountability language). (3) Peer bank universality: every major UK bank faces this problem; it is not a firm-specific design choice. Passes tests: incompatible incentives, regulatory inscription, peer bank universality.
 
**UK-specific manifestation.** When a payment to a sanctioned entity is made due to a screening gap, the CRO writes it up as "operational risk event — sanctions screening failure — severity 3"; the MLRO writes it up as "OFSI breach — strict liability — mandatory notification"; the process owner writes it up as "exception — payment held pending investigation — cleared"; the SMF17 documents it as "reasonable steps taken — screening configuration reviewed, OFSI notified, remediation plan initiated." Four documents, one event, no common data record. The MLRO's OFSI notification is filed on the regulatory record; the CRO's operational risk event sits in the risk framework. The platform attempting to give a unified view of this event must translate across all four languages in real time.
 
**Regulatory inscription.** SYSC 6.3 (FCA Handbook obligation language); PRA ERMF / ICAAP guidance (risk taxonomy); SMCR FSMA s.66A (personal accountability language); operational SLAs embedded in outsourcing contracts and internal KPI frameworks.
 
**What a platform must do with this friction.** Maintain a unified ontology that automatically generates all four views from a single event record. Every event must carry metadata enabling it to be surfaced as: (a) the obligation it relates to; (b) the risk taxonomy node it triggers; (c) the process step it touches; (d) the SMF whose SoR it falls under and what reasonable-steps evidence it generates. The platform does not resolve the language problem; it provides a single data backbone from which each stakeholder can extract their required view.

### F.2 The Four-Way Ownership Friction
 
**Friction name and description.** For any material control in a UK bank, accountability is distributed across four roles: (1) the Process Owner who owns the workflow; (2) the Control Owner (typically second-line) who owns the control design and standards; (3) the Control Operator who executes the control (often an offshore team); (4) the accountable SMF whose Statement of Responsibilities covers the regulatory obligation the control satisfies. These four roles report to different management lines, are measured on different KPIs, and bear different types of accountability. The SMF bears statutory personal liability. The control operator in Hyderabad bears operational performance accountability. These are not compatible accountability structures.
 
**Why it is structural — tests passed.** (1) Incompatible incentives: throughput (process owner), control integrity (control owner), execution speed (operator), personal regulatory liability avoidance (SMF). (2) Regulatory inscription: SMCR is statutory (FSMA s.66A); 3LoD independence is mandated in FCA SYSC / PRA Rulebook; the offshore captive execution model is embedded in operational strategy that would require restructuring of the business to eliminate. (3) Authority graph failure: fixing a failure requires coordination across all four roles and their respective management lines. (4) Peer bank universality: the 3LoD mandate combined with SMF personal accountability applies to every dual-regulated firm; the combination is unique to the UK SMCR framework. Passes tests: incompatible incentives, regulatory inscription, authority graph complexity, peer bank universality.
 
**UK-specific manifestation.** A sanctions screening miss occurs at 11pm on a Tuesday. The CHAPS payment has already settled. The process owner (Head of Payment Ops) owns the workflow; they ask their team to investigate. The control owner (Head of Sanctions Compliance) owns the screening configuration; they document the configuration gap. The control operator (India captive screening team) executed the check; their supervisor escalates to the UK overnight manager. SMF17 (MLRO) has a strict liability breach under ECTEA 2022; they must file with OFSI within a reasonable timeframe and prepare a personal reasonable-steps evidence record. Four simultaneous response tracks, no single incident commander, and the SMF17's evidence file must be assembled from the outputs of three other functions over which they have no direct authority.
 
**Regulatory inscription.** FSMA s.66A (SMF personal accountability); SYSC 4.7 and SYSC 6 (3LoD independence); PRA SS1/21 (OpRes ownership — PR Z must be allocated to a named SMF); ECTEA 2022 (strict liability for sanctions breaches making ownership resolution time-critical).
 
**What a platform must do with this friction.** The RCM must carry an "SMF Accountable" column separate from "Control Owner" and "Control Operator." When an incident occurs, the platform must simultaneously notify all four accountability layers with role-specific framing: the process owner receives an operational alert with SLA implications; the control owner receives a control failure notification with remediation requirements; the control operator receives a case event; the accountable SMF receives a personal evidence record creation request with the Reasonable Steps evidence prompt. The platform does not resolve the accountability structure; it surfaces it immediately and in parallel.

### F.3 Documentation–Reality Drift (OpRes Mapping Specific)
 
**Friction name and description.** Operational Resilience requires annual board-approved self-assessments built on IBS mapping. The mapping is a point-in-time document. The business changes continuously — technology migrations, new outsourcing arrangements, headcount restructures, new products. The self-assessment that the board approves in Q1 accurately describes the firm as it existed when the mapping was done; by Q3 it may materially diverge from operational reality. This gap is structurally inherent because the governance cadence (annual approval, quarterly committee oversight) operates at a fundamentally slower cycle than the operational change cadence (continuous).
 
**Why it is structural — tests passed.** (1) Time-scale mismatch: governance operates annually or quarterly; operational change operates weekly or daily. (2) Authority graph: refreshing the IBS mapping requires coordinating IT (CMDB), Business Continuity, Third-Party Risk Management, and the OpRes function — four separate teams. (3) Regulatory inscription: the annual self-assessment is a hard regulatory requirement (PS21/3 / SS1/21); it is not possible to increase approval frequency indefinitely without the governance process becoming meaningless. (4) Reversibility test: eliminating the drift would require either continuous real-time board approval (operationally impossible) or a different regulatory framework (requires regulatory change). Passes tests: time-scale mismatch, authority graph complexity, regulatory inscription, reversibility failure.
 
**UK-specific manifestation.** A mid-sized UK bank completes its Operational Resilience self-assessment in February 2025, mapping the CHAPS payment IBS to its legacy on-premise payment hub. In June 2025, the payment hub is migrated to a cloud-based deployment on AWS as part of the bank's infrastructure modernisation programme. The IBS mapping in the self-assessment is now incorrect. The Bank's CMDB is updated within two weeks of the migration, but the OpRes function's tool, which draws on a quarterly CMDB extract, is not updated until September 2025. When the FCA conducts a supervisory visit in October 2025 and asks to review the CHAPS IBS mapping, it reflects a system that no longer exists. The self-assessment that the board approved in February 2025 is demonstrably inaccurate. The bank must remediate — and the SMF with the OpRes Prescribed Responsibility (PR Z) must demonstrate the reasonable steps they took to maintain mapping currency.
 
**Regulatory inscription.** PS21/3 / SS1/21 paragraph 7 (annual self-assessment requirement); PRA SS2/21 (material outsourcing notification, which should trigger mapping update); PRA / FCA joint supervisory statement on operational change management within OpRes framework (supervisory expectation that material changes trigger IBS review).
 
**What a platform must do with this friction.** Derive IBS mapping from system telemetry (CMDB, change management logs, incident records) rather than relying on periodic manual updates. Reconcile the derived mapping against the documented self-assessment continuously. Surface misalignments immediately as a mapping-drift alert. Trigger a "material change review" workflow when a change management event (new outsourcing, technology migration, headcount restructure above a defined threshold) is detected. This does not eliminate the annual board-approval cycle — it ensures the document the board approves accurately reflects current state.

### F.4 Speed–Assurance Tension
 
**Friction name and description.** UK banking simultaneously operates sub-second transaction execution (Faster Payments) and time-consuming assurance processes (DISP 8-week complaint investigation, OpRes impact tolerance documentation, APP fraud reimbursement claims). The compliance standards that apply to real-time payment execution — sanctions screening, CoP, fraud scoring, AML monitoring — must produce decisions in milliseconds. The evidential standard that applies to those decisions — what a regulator or skilled person expects to see — requires structured, documented, defensible reasoning that takes minutes or hours to produce. These two requirements are irreconcilable within the same operational moment.
 
**Why it is structural — tests passed.** (1) Incompatible incentives: the payment execution function is rewarded for speed; the compliance function is rewarded for assurance quality. (2) Time-scale mismatch: millisecond execution versus week-long investigation. (3) Regulatory inscription: PSRs 2017 Reg 86 (FPS near-real-time execution) and PS21/3 IBS impact tolerance for Payments are simultaneously mandatory with DISP 8-week deadline and APP reimbursement 5-business-day decision requirement. (4) Reversibility: removing speed would destroy the product; removing assurance would breach regulation. Passes tests: incompatible incentives, time-scale mismatch, regulatory inscription, reversibility failure.
 
**UK-specific manifestation.** A customer initiates a £150,000 CHAPS payment on a Friday afternoon. The CoP check returns a "close match" — the beneficiary name is slightly different from the registered account name. The fraud score is elevated — the payment is larger than the customer's usual profile and the beneficiary is a newly registered company. The relationship manager receives an automated hold notification. The bank must decide whether to release or hold the payment in less than 3 minutes (the CHAPS same-day cut-off is approaching). If held pending investigation, the customer relationship is at risk. If released and the payment is fraudulent, the bank bears 50% APP fraud liability under PSR PS23/3. The assurance standard (demonstrate a defensible decision with documented reasoning) and the speed standard (decide in 3 minutes) cannot both be fully met. This tension is repeated thousands of times per day across the UK banking system and has no systematic resolution.
 
**Regulatory inscription.** PSRs 2017 Reg 86 (FPS real-time execution); PSR PS23/3 (APP fraud reimbursement creating liability for payment decisions); DISP 1.6.2R (8-week complaint investigation); OBL-OFSI-001 (sanctions screening before release); SYSC 15A / PS21/3 (impact tolerance for the Payments IBS requiring continuous operation).
 
**What a platform must do with this friction.** Support a pre-computed risk score that is continuously updated between transactions, allowing the millisecond decision to draw on a richer evidence base built over time. Simultaneously build an audit log of the decision that records the risk score, the inputs, the threshold applied, and the disposition — creating evidence at the speed of the transaction. Surface the tension explicitly to the relationship manager at the hold-or-release decision point, giving them the regulatory consequence of each option, not just the commercial outcome. Ensure the post-decision investigation capability exists so that when a released payment later proves fraudulent, the evidence of the original decision is available for the APP fraud reimbursement assessment.

### F.5 The Reactive Accountability Cycle
 
**Friction name and description.** FCA and PRA thematic reviews and enforcement actions examine historical practice — typically the 12–24 months preceding the review. Banks are held accountable for conduct that, at the time of review, they have already identified and remediated. The Consumer Duty review conducted by the FCA in early 2024 examined firm behaviour from mid-2023 onward, before most banks had embedded Consumer Duty operationally. Banks that acted quickly to remediate early gaps were still examined on the basis of their remediation period, not their current state. The regulatory process is inherently backward-looking; the bank's operations are continuously forward-moving. This gap cannot be closed.
 
**Why it is structural — tests passed.** (1) Time-scale mismatch: the regulatory examination cycle (12–24 month lookback) is structurally slower than the operational improvement cycle (continuous). (2) Regulatory inscription: FSMA powers (s.165, s.166) give the FCA/PRA access to historical records; enforcement actions are built on historical evidence; the legal requirement to maintain records for 5+ years means historical accountability persists. (3) Peer bank universality: every bank operating under FCA/PRA supervision faces this cycle; it is not firm-specific. (4) Reversibility failure: the regulatory framework requires backward-looking examination for accountability to be meaningful; making it forward-looking only would eliminate the deterrent effect. Passes tests: time-scale mismatch, regulatory inscription, peer bank universality, reversibility failure.
 
**UK-specific manifestation.** A mid-sized UK bank identified weaknesses in its Consumer Duty FVA methodology in August 2023 and began a remediation programme. By January 2024, the remediation was substantially complete and the methodology had been strengthened. In June 2024, an FCA portfolio letter references the bank's Consumer Duty implementation as requiring enhancement — it is examining the period from July 2023 to March 2024, which is the remediation period. The bank is held accountable for activity during a period it was actively remediating. The compliance team must now document not only current state (which is improved) but also explain the evolution from prior state — including why the gap existed and what evidence exists that reasonable steps were being taken to close it. The backward-looking accountability cycle means that a good remediation programme generates its own evidentiary exposure.
 
**Regulatory inscription.** FSMA s.165 (regulators' power to require information on any historical matter within the retention period); MLR 2017 Reg 40 (5-year record retention); DISP 1.10R (FCA use of complaints data, which reflects historical behaviour); FCA Enforcement Guide (investigations typically cover historical periods of 1–5 years).
 
**What a platform must do with this friction.** Support historical state queries as a native capability — the ability to reconstruct "what was the control configuration, the obligation mapping, and the evidence state on date X?" This requires time-stamped versioning of every substantive record. Every change to a control design, a risk appetite setting, or an obligation mapping must be preserved with the previous state. When a regulatory inquiry arrives covering a historical period, the platform must support evidence extraction that reflects the state of the firm during that period, not the current state.

### F.6 The Geographic-Structural Friction (Ring-Fencing, Offshore Execution, Post-Brexit Divergence)
 
**Friction name and description.** UK banking faces three simultaneous geographic structural tensions: (1) Ring-fencing — the RFB/NRFB split requires operationally separate entities with independent governance, creating a compliance boundary within the same group that must be policed; (2) Offshore captive execution — UK-regulated processes are executed by offshore captive teams (typically India, Philippines, South Africa) under UK SMCR accountability held onshore; (3) Post-Brexit parallel stacks — banks with both UK and EU operations must maintain separate UK GDPR + UK EMIR + UK MiFIR compliance and EU GDPR + EU EMIR + EU MiFIR compliance, without cross-contamination.
 
**Why it is structural — tests passed.** (1) Regulatory inscription: ring-fencing is statutory (Banking Reform Act 2013 / FSMA Part 9B); offshore execution is embedded in long-term operational strategy requiring multi-year restructuring to unwind; post-Brexit divergence is a geopolitical outcome baked into UK regulatory law. (2) Authority graph: the ring-fencing boundary creates coordination friction between the RFB compliance function and the group compliance function; the offshore captive creates a different authority structure for quality oversight; the post-Brexit divergence creates two parallel regulatory monitoring obligations. (3) Peer bank universality: every major UK bank with international operations and offshore captives faces all three simultaneously. (4) Budget test: no amount of compliance investment eliminates these structural features — they are definitional. Passes tests: regulatory inscription, authority graph complexity, peer bank universality, counterfactual budget failure.
 
**UK-specific manifestation.** An AML investigation concerning a corporate customer is initiated by the UK financial crime team in London. The case investigation is executed by a team in Bangalore. The customer has accounts in both the RFB and the NRFB. The RFB's MLRO (SMF17, onshore) is responsible for the SAR decision. The NRFB's separate governance committee needs to be notified because the customer also operates through the NRFB. The investigation team in Bangalore has access to UK systems for the RFB but not the NRFB — a ring-fencing data-access boundary applies. The customer also has a loan through the bank's Irish subsidiary (EU-regulated) where the SAR process operates under different rules. Four simultaneous governance tracks, three jurisdictions, two legal entities within the same group, and one offshore execution team. The SMF17 in London must evidence reasonable steps across all of it.
 
**Regulatory inscription.** FSMA Part 9B and Ring-Fenced Bodies Order (ring-fencing); MLR 2017 Reg 20 (group-wide AML applying to branches and subsidiaries, triggering group co-ordination need); PRA SS2/21 (material outsourcing — offshore captive operations are usually structured as intra-group outsourcing); UK GDPR / UK EMIR / UK MiFIR post-Brexit retention (divergence from EU equivalents embedded in domestic law).
 
**What a platform must do with this friction.** Maintain jurisdiction-aware evidence architecture: every control record, every obligation mapping, every incident record must carry a jurisdiction tag (UK RFB, UK NRFB, EU, other) and a legal-entity attribute. The evidence available to support a given investigation must respect ring-fencing data boundaries — the platform cannot allow the RFB evidence archive to commingle with the NRFB evidence archive without appropriate governance. The offshore execution view must separately surface UK-SMF oversight requirements and the quality assurance trail from offshore to onshore. Post-Brexit compliance stacks are maintained as parallel configurations with separate obligation registers and separate evidence archives, linked through shared entity-level customer records where permitted.

## SECTION G — Key UK–US Divergences
 
The following 30 specific divergences represent substantively different regulatory architecture — not relabelled, genuinely different. Each is one sentence, specific and traceable.
 
1. **SMF pre-approval is statutory:** UK regulators must approve senior managers before they begin their function (FSMA s.59); US banks appoint senior management without equivalent pre-approval from the OCC, Fed, or FDIC.
2. **Personal liability is direct and individual:** UK SMCR imposes direct personal regulatory liability on named SMFs through the "reasonable steps" standard (FSMA s.66A); US heightened standards create institutional obligations with no equivalent named-individual personal liability structure.
3. **Principles-based versus rules-based supervision:** UK FCA supervision assesses outcomes against Principles (PRIN); US OCC/Fed supervision operates primarily against a rulebook where specific rule adherence is tested and outcomes are inferred.
4. **Compliance moment of truth:** UK's compliance examination instrument is the s.166 Skilled Persons Review (firm-paid, regulator-scoped, skilled-person-executed, outcome published); US uses agency-run examinations (government-funded, agency-scoped, findings confidential to the firm except in enforcement actions).
5. **Consumer Duty as an outcomes obligation:** UK Consumer Duty (PRIN 12 / PS22/9) requires documented evidence of good customer outcomes; US UDAAP is a conduct-based prohibition without an equivalent outcomes-monitoring architecture.
6. **Vulnerable customer as an explicit operational requirement:** FCA FG21/1 creates a detailed operational obligation to identify and respond to vulnerability; US lacks a unified equivalent framework applicable to all banking products.
7. **APP fraud mandatory reimbursement:** UK PSR PS23/3 creates mandatory reimbursement up to £85,000 with a 50/50 sending/receiving PSP liability split; US has no equivalent mandatory APP fraud reimbursement regime.
8. **Confirmation of Payee as a regulatory mandate:** UK CoP is a PSR-directed name-matching check mandatory for in-scope payments with a prescribed customer disclosure format; US has no equivalent national name-matching service.
9. **SAR consent mechanism (DAML):** UK POCA 2002 ss.335–336 create a Defensive Against Money Laundering regime where the firm requests NCA consent to transact, with a 7 working day notice period and 31 calendar day moratorium; US SAR filing has no equivalent consent mechanism.
10. **Strict liability for sanctions post-ECTEA 2022:** UK OFSI can impose civil penalties for sanctions breaches without needing to establish knowledge or reasonable cause to suspect (since June 2022); US OFAC's enforcement framework requires knowledge or reason to know for the most severe penalties.
11. **Annual MLRO report as a named individual's regulatory artefact:** UK SYSC 6.3.6R requires the named MLRO to produce and sign an annual report for senior management assessing AML systems effectiveness; US BSA has no equivalent named-individual annual report obligation.
12. **Domestic PEPs included explicitly:** UK MLR 2017 Reg 35 includes domestic (UK) PEPs and their families; historically US focus has been on foreign PEPs under 31 CFR Part 1010.
13. **Proliferation financing as a standalone risk assessment requirement:** UK MLR 2017 (as amended 2022) requires a separate proliferation financing risk assessment; US BSA/AML rules do not yet contain an equivalent freestanding obligation at firm level.
14. **Ring-fencing as structural separation:** UK Banking Reform Act 2013 mandates physical, legal, and operational separation of the RFB and NRFB; US Volcker Rule is an activity restriction within a single entity, not a structural separation requirement.
15. **Operational Resilience IBS framework:** UK PS21/3 / SS1/21 introduce the Important Business Services construct with quantified impact tolerances and severe-but-plausible scenario testing; US FFIEC BCM Handbook addresses business continuity without the specific IBS quantified-tolerance architecture.
16. **Material outsourcing pre-notification to regulator:** UK PRA SS2/21 requires firms to notify the PRA before entering a material outsourcing arrangement; US OCC 2023-17 uses a risk-based approach without equivalent pre-notification.
17. **Critical Third Parties direct regulatory oversight:** FSMA 2023 gives UK regulators direct oversight powers over designated CTPs (cloud providers, key infrastructure); US has no equivalent direct CTP regulatory designation regime operational as of 2025.
18. **UK EMIR Refit divergence from EU timing:** UK EMIR Refit went live 30 September 2024 (6 months after EU, April 2024), with distinct ISO 20022 field requirements; US Title VII Dodd-Frank derivatives reporting operates under a separate CFTC framework with different fields and timing.
19. **Statement of Responsibilities as a personal regulatory document:** UK SoR is submitted to regulators, forms the basis of personal liability, and must be updated on material change (SYSC 25.4); US bank officers do not submit equivalent personal accountability documents to their federal regulator.
20. **Management Responsibilities Map as a firm-level accountability document:** UK MRM is a single regulator-reviewable document showing all SMF accountability; US banks have no equivalent unified regulatory accountability map submission.
21. **Conduct Rules applying to near-all bank staff including offshore captive:** UK COCON Individual Conduct Rules apply to virtually all in-scope bank employees including those in offshore captive centres executing UK-regulated processes; US does not have an equivalent universal individual conduct standard with regulator-notification requirements.
22. **Annual Fitness and Propriety certification for significant-harm functions:** UK Certification Regime requires annual F&P assessment and certificate issuance for all Certified Persons; US has no equivalent annual fitness-and-propriety certification process at this population scale.
23. **FOS as a binding adjudicator up to £415,000:** UK Financial Ombudsman Service resolves complaints up to £415,000 and its decisions are binding on the firm; US CFPB and FINRA equivalents operate differently in binding authority and monetary limits.
24. **Faster Payments near-real-time execution as a regulatory standard:** UK PSRs 2017 Reg 86 mandates near-real-time execution for FPS payments; US FedNow operates under different design parameters and voluntary adoption.
25. **Post-Brexit UK GDPR as a distinct jurisdiction:** UK GDPR diverges from EU GDPR in adequacy decisions, international transfers framework, and ICO enforcement approach; US has no equivalent unified federal privacy framework applicable to banking.
26. **DPA 2018 Schedule 2 financial crime exemption:** The specific UK domestic framing of GDPR Article 23 derogations in Schedule 2 DPA 2018 for crime prevention creates a distinct banking exemption architecture not replicated in US federal privacy law.
27. **FCA Complaints Return and public disclosure:** UK DISP 1.10 requires bi-annual aggregated complaints returns published by the FCA with firm-level data; US has no equivalent public, firm-identified complaints performance database.
28. **Dual-regulator tension within a single firm:** UK banks are simultaneously supervised by PRA (prudential) and FCA (conduct) with independent powers and occasionally conflicting supervisory postures over the same risk; US supervision is distributed across federal/state lines with different functional split.
29. **JMLSG guidance as HMT-approved persuasive standard:** JMLSG guidance is approved by HM Treasury and carries "persuasive weight" before courts (MLR 2017 Reg 86 equivalent); US has no semi-binding industry guidance at this level — industry standards like Wolfsberg are persuasive but without a statutory HMT-equivalent endorsement.
30. **SMF4 CRO ICAAP accountability is personal:** The UK CRO (SMF4) personally attests to the adequacy of the ICAAP and faces personal regulatory consequences if the ICAAP is found inadequate; US CCAR/stress testing obligations are institutional with no equivalent SMF-specific personal attestation liability.
